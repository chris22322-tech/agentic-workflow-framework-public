# Automate the repetitive parts of your job. Keep the parts that need you.

You have work that follows the same shape every time — pulling data from three systems, writing it up, handing it off to someone who needs to act on it. This framework walks you through turning workflows like that into AI agents that do the repetitive parts for you, while you review what matters.

[:material-arrow-right: Start with Stage 1](stages/01-decompose.md){ .md-button .md-button--primary }

---

## One concrete example

A Customer Success Manager runs a **quarterly account health review** — pulling data from the CRM, support platform, and usage metrics; assessing each signal; writing a report; flagging risks. It takes about six hours per account, and a strategic portfolio of ten accounts eats sixty hours a quarter.

With an agent built using this framework, most of that work happens automatically. The CSM spends about an hour reviewing the draft, correcting anything the agent got wrong, and approving it. The other five hours per account come back.

The agent doesn't replace the judgement. It removes the data-gathering and first-draft-writing that eats the time.

## How it works

You map your workflow in plain language — what triggers it, what data you use, what you produce, and where you need to stay in the loop. The framework turns your map into a design an engineer can build from, or that you can build yourself using a low-code agent builder.

**You don't need to code to start.** The first three stages are thinking work: understanding your workflows, picking the right one, mapping it out. You can start right now with a blank page.

## Before you start: how to notice the workflows you don't know you have

The hardest part isn't scoping a workflow — it's **noticing** which parts of your week are repetitive in the first place. People are blind to patterns in their own work because they've internalised them. Four techniques that reliably surface workflows you've never written down:

- **The Tuesday morning audit.** Describe your last Tuesday morning out loud or in a note, from arrival to lunch. Every concrete activity you mention is a candidate. You'll find three or four you never thought to list when someone asked *"what are your workflows?"*
- **The sabbatical test.** Imagine you're away for three months. What would you need to hand over to a colleague covering for you? Each item on that handover list is a workflow — by definition it has structure, because someone else has to learn it.
- **The recurring email search.** Search your sent folder for phrases you type often (*"attached is"*, *"here's the latest"*, *"as discussed"*). Each hit is a recurring deliverable, which means a recurring workflow sits behind it.
- **The Friday pile-up.** If you took a Friday off, what accumulates by Monday morning? The pile is almost entirely repetitive workflow work that only *you* normally handle — it's a map of everything you haven't delegated or automated.

!!! tip "Spend 15 minutes on this before you open Stage 1"
    The framework is excellent at scoping workflows you already know are hard. But some of its biggest wins come from workflows you only notice once you go looking. One or two of the techniques above, on a blank page, takes a quarter of an hour and reliably surfaces 3-5 candidates that were hiding in plain sight.
