# Worked Example: Stage 3 -- Scope

!!! example "Worked Example"
    We're applying Stage 3 to: **Quarterly Account Health Review**. The goal is to map every step of the workflow as a human currently performs it, then draw the automation boundary.

!!! note "Multi-product accounts: a deliberate v1 simplification"
    Many enterprise accounts have multiple licensed products or modules. Step 3 pulls usage metrics at the account level, and Step 7 assesses engagement against a single set of tier benchmarks. This means accounts with multiple products will show blended metrics — a healthy product can mask a declining one. This is a known limitation, not an oversight. V1 aggregates usage at the account level as a deliberate simplification; per-product health scoring is deferred to v2. The decision to aggregate is stated here as a constraint so it can be validated during evaluation (see [Test Case 5 in Stage 6](evaluate.md)), not discovered as a surprise. If per-product health visibility is critical for your portfolio, consider scoping a product-level agent variant.

!!! note "Stakeholder sentiment: per-role map vs. single composite score"
    A CSM's real mental model is not "overall sentiment is mixed" — it is "the champion is happy but the economic buyer is going cold." Strategic and mid-market accounts typically have 3–5 stakeholders with divergent sentiment, and a single composite reading loses the signal that drives action. Step 5's output now specifies a per-stakeholder-role sentiment map (executive sponsor, champion, end users) with a composite rollup, and the decision logic weights by stakeholder seniority. However, the downstream health scoring in Step 9 still consumes sentiment as a single dimension (Green/Amber/Red). V1 produces the per-role breakdown to give the CSM actionable context, but the scoring rubric applies the composite rollup. V2 should incorporate stakeholder-level sentiment into the scoring framework — for example, executive sponsor sentiment as its own scored dimension — so that "champion happy, economic buyer cold" produces a different health score than "everyone mildly dissatisfied." If your accounts have complex buying committees, validate during evaluation that the per-role map is surfacing role-level divergence the CSM would otherwise miss (see [Test Case 2 in Stage 6](evaluate.md)).

### Error Path Register

The workflow map above captures what each step does when it succeeds. The register below captures what happens when a step fails — specifically, which downstream steps are affected and how the workflow degrades. One row per failure condition, not per step.

| Step | Failure Category | Error Condition | Downstream Impact | Fallback Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 — Pull CRM account data | Data malformed | ARR, primary contact, or contract dates missing or stale in CRM record | Step 7 cannot select correct tier benchmarks without ARR (enterprise vs mid-market vs SMB thresholds differ). Step 8 cannot assess renewal risk without contract dates. Step 8b cannot map action urgency without renewal proximity. Step 9 health score may apply wrong tier thresholds and cannot weight contract risk | Flag each missing field explicitly (e.g., mark ARR as missing with a note: "ARR not recorded in CRM"). Downstream steps that depend on the missing field note the gap rather than inferring -- Step 7 reports "tier benchmarks not applied: ARR unavailable" rather than defaulting to a tier |
| 1b — Pull CS platform health score | Data unavailable | CS platform API unavailable, or organisation does not use a CS platform | Step 9 cannot perform platform score reconciliation — the CSM loses the divergence signal that reveals where structured metrics and qualitative reality have parted ways | Return null gracefully. Step 9 skips reconciliation and scores using agent-gathered data only. Note in report that no platform comparison was available |
| 1c — Pull prior quarter's health report | Data unavailable | No prior report exists (first review cycle for this account) or output store is unreachable | Step 7b cannot assess action-item follow-through — the agent loses the longitudinal feedback loop that distinguishes "new problem" from "recurring problem where prior intervention failed." Steps 8 and 8b proceed without carry-forward context. Step 9 health score is missing the ticket dimension entirely. Step 10 report has a blank ticket health section | Flag gap in state. Step 6 outputs "ticket data unavailable — manual review recommended." Step 9 scores on remaining three dimensions (usage, sentiment, contract) and notes the missing dimension. Step 10 inserts a gap notice in the ticket section rather than omitting it silently |
| 3 — Pull product usage metrics | Data unavailable | Usage API or data warehouse query fails or returns no data for the account | Step 7 (engagement assessment) cannot execute — no DAU/MAU, feature adoption, or API call volume available. Step 9 health score is missing the usage dimension. Step 8 risk identification loses the "declining usage + upcoming renewal = churn risk" signal | Flag gap in state. Step 7 outputs "usage data unavailable — manual review recommended." Step 9 scores on remaining dimensions and notes the gap. The report makes the absence visible so the CSM can investigate whether the data source is misconfigured or the account genuinely has no usage |
| 4 — Pull recent comms context | Data unavailable | No data returns from any configured channel (Slack, email, call recordings, CS platform notes) for the account in the target period | Step 5 (sentiment analysis) has no input to analyse — the qualitative dimension that contextualises quantitative scores is absent. Steps 8 and 9 lose the sentiment signal that the scoring rubric uses to weight risk | Mark comms context as empty. Step 5 outputs "insufficient data for sentiment assessment" rather than inferring from absence. Step 9 scores sentiment as "not assessed" and notes it in the report. | 6 — Assess ticket health | Data malformed | SLA compliance calculation returns an impossible value (>100% or negative), indicating a formula error or upstream data corruption | Step 9 ingests a nonsensical ticket health metric. Step 10 report presents a number that misleads the CSM and any stakeholders who receive it | Reject the calculation, log the anomaly, and flag "SLA compliance metric requires manual verification" rather than passing a bad number downstream. Step 9 scores the ticket dimension without the SLA compliance component and notes the gap |
| 7 — Assess product engagement | Data malformed | Usage metrics appear below benchmark due to data quality issues (incomplete extraction, changed tracking schema) rather than actual usage decline | Step 8 flags a false-positive churn risk. Step 8b generates unnecessary intervention actions. Step 9 assigns a lower health score than warranted, potentially triggering escalation for a healthy account | Step 7's HIL checkpoint (triggered on edge cases) catches this — the CSM who knows the account can distinguish genuine decline from data artefacts. | 7b — Assess prior action-item follow-through | Data malformed | Prior report's action items lack completion status annotations — the document exists but was not updated with outcomes, so the agent cannot determine which items were completed | Step 7b produces an unreliable completion rate. Step 8b may carry forward items that were actually completed, or miss genuinely unresolved commitments | Flag that completion status data is incomplete. | 9 — Assign overall health score | Human rejects | CSM disagrees with the proposed health rating after reviewing component scores and justification | Step 10 report was drafted using the original rating — narrative framing, risk emphasis, and action-item urgency may be inconsistent with the CSM's revised score | Return to Step 9 with the CSM's revised rating and justification. Regenerate Steps 10 and 11 using the updated score to ensure the report narrative and exec summary reflect the corrected assessment |
| 12b — Write back to CS platform | API failure | CS platform API returns error, times out, or rejects the write (e.g., permission denied, field validation failure, rate limit exceeded) | The system of record retains the prior quarter's stale health score. Never silently swallow a write-back failure — a stale system of record is the exact problem this step exists to prevent |
| 12b — Write back to CS platform | Data mapping failure | The agent's health score format (Green/Amber/Red) does not map to the platform's native score schema (e.g., platform uses 0-100 numeric scale and the mapping config is missing or misconfigured) | The write either fails validation on the platform side or writes a meaningless value | Validate the score mapping during agent setup, not at runtime. If the mapping is missing at execution time, block the write and surface the configuration error to the CSM rather than writing an unmapped value. Include the expected mapping format in the error message so the ops team can fix the config |

!!! note "Data quality validation belongs at the source, not downstream"
    Validate data completeness at the data-gathering step (Step 1) before passing data to analysis steps. Missing fields should produce an explicit gap marker -- for example, if ARR is not recorded in the CRM, the agent should flag "ARR not recorded" rather than silently treating it as zero. This is a common pattern: most enterprise CRM instances have data quality issues (incomplete fields, stale records, duplicated accounts), and the agent needs to be honest about what it does not know rather than inferring from incomplete data. A health score built on missing ARR and outdated contacts is worse than a health score that says "insufficient data to score reliably."

### Integration Requirements

The following integration requirements are for the engineer who will build the agent. As the workflow owner, validate the data sources above -- the technical choices below are for your engineering partner.

- **Agent framework or platform** — whichever your team has chosen (see [Choose a Platform](../getting-started/choose-a-platform.md))
- **Tool wrappers** — one per data source in the inventory above, wired to whatever your platform supports (connector catalogues, API specs, custom functions, direct SDK calls)
- **Language model provider** — for the synthesis, summarisation, and report generation reasoning steps
- **File output** — Markdown or HTML report generation
- **Credentials management** -- Environment variables or secrets manager for API keys/tokens

### Constraints and Assumptions

- **Account identity must resolve consistently across systems.** The workflow assumes each step can look up the same account across CRM, support, usage, comms, and billing systems using a shared identifier. In practice, account names and IDs are rarely consistent — `Globex Inc` in Salesforce, `Globex` in Zendesk, `globex-inc` in the usage platform. Before the agent can reliably pull data across sources, the CSM (or their ops team) must create and validate an **account-to-system-ID mapping table**: one row per account, one column per system, containing the system-specific identifier. This is non-trivial setup work — expect to spend time on manual matching, deduplication, and handling subsidiary/parent relationships. Treat the mapping table as a prerequisite artefact, not a nice-to-have. Without it, Steps 1–4 will silently return incomplete data or match the wrong records, and every downstream assessment inherits that error. If your organisation already has a master data management (MDM) layer or a unified customer ID across systems, you can skip this step — but verify that the unified ID actually resolves in every system the agent queries, not just the CRM

    !!! warning "Assess this before you score Feasibility in Stage 2"
        If your organisation does not have a unified customer ID or master data management layer, factor in the effort to build an account-to-system-ID mapping table **before** scoring Feasibility. This prerequisite work can take 1–2 days per 30 accounts and must be maintained as accounts are added. Most CSM orgs have inconsistent naming across Salesforce, Zendesk, usage platforms, and Slack — and the mapping work is manual, tedious, and requires the CSM (not an engineer) to validate. If your feasibility score assumed clean cross-system identity resolution and you do not have it, revisit that score. This is the single most common blocker for cross-system CS automation.

- All data sources have programmatic access (API or query interface)
- The agent will not have direct access to send the final report -- distribution remains manual. However, once the CSM approves the report in Step 12, the agent writes the approved health score and key findings back to the CS platform in Step 12b — this is a system-of-record update, not distribution
- Communication channel mix varies by account (Slack, email, calls, Teams, shared support channels). The agent should query all configured sources and work with whatever returns data rather than failing when a source is absent. During onboarding, the CSM should indicate which channels are active for each account
- Comms search results (especially Slack and email) may contain noise; the agent should over-retrieve and let the human filter during the HIL step
- The health scoring framework is defined and stable (if it changes frequently, it should be externalised as config, not hardcoded)
- **Workflow depth varies by account tier.** Not every account warrants the full 17-step deep-dive. For scaled portfolios, the workflow should be configurable by account segment so that CSMs are not spending 45 minutes reviewing a $30K mid-market account the same way they review a $1.2M enterprise renewal. A practical default:

    | Tier | Steps Executed | HIL Behaviour |
    |---|---|---|
    | **Strategic** (top-tier / high-ARR / complex) | Full 17-step flow | All HIL checkpoints active |
    | **Core** (mid-market / standard complexity) | Full 17-step flow | Step 5 (comms sentiment) HIL triggers only if the automated sentiment score is Amber or Red; Green scores proceed without pause |
    | **Scaled** (long-tail / low-touch) | Steps 1-4, 6-7b, 9-12b (skip Step 5 comms sentiment HIL, skip Step 8b action-item generation) | HIL triggers only at Step 9 (health score) and only if any dimension scores Red |

    Implement this as a `tier` field on the account config that maps to a tier profile in the scoring rubric. The tier profile controls two things: (1) which steps are included in the workflow, and (2) the threshold at which each HIL checkpoint activates.

    The tier assignment itself is metadata pulled from the CRM in Step 1 (the `tier` field already exists in the account metadata output). If your organisation does not segment accounts, treat every account as Strategic — the full flow is the safe default.

    ??? note "Technical detail: tier configuration (for engineers)"

        Example rubric config fragment:

        ```yaml
        tier_profiles:
        ```

- **V1 aggregates product usage metrics at the account level.** Accounts with multiple products will show blended metrics. Per-product decomposition is deferred to v2 — if product-level health visibility is critical for your portfolio, consider scoping a product-level agent variant. This is a deliberate simplification: the blended approach keeps the agent, its prompts, and the report structure tractable for a first build, at the cost of masking per-product divergence
- The agent operates per-account; batch processing (all accounts at once) is a future enhancement

- **Data sensitivity / governance:** The agent sends verbatim client data to an external LLM API for synthesis, summarisation, and report generation. The data categories sent to the LLM include: client ARR and contract renewal dates (Steps 1, 1b), support ticket content including CSAT scores and resolution details (Step 2), product usage metrics and adoption trends (Step 3), Slack messages which may contain deal terms, pricing discussions, and contract negotiations (Step 4), NPS and relationship survey responses with verbatim client comments (Step 1c), call recording summaries with client sentiment (Step 4), and CRM account metadata including segmentation, tier, and CSM notes (Step 1). In aggregate, this constitutes commercially sensitive client financial data, personal communications, and relationship intelligence — categories that most organisations classify as Confidential or Restricted. **Governance prerequisites before build:** (1) Confirm the organisation has an approved AI data processing policy that permits sending these data categories to an external LLM provider. (2) Review client contracts for third-party data processing restrictions — some enterprise clients have contractual clauses prohibiting sharing their data with sub-processors without explicit consent. (3) Complete the third-party risk assessment for the chosen LLM provider. (4) Determine whether the organisation's model risk management (MRM) framework applies to the health scoring and risk assessment outputs, given that they inform business decisions (renewal strategy, escalation). (5) If the workflow processes data from clients in GDPR-regulated jurisdictions, initiate a Data Processing Impact Assessment (DPIA). See the [Compliance Readiness pre-flight checklist](../stages/03-scope.md#compliance-readiness-start-early) in the methodology — these conversations should start during Stage 1 (Decompose), not be discovered at deployment.

    !!! warning "In regulated industries, this is the constraint most likely to block deployment"
        A technically complete agent that has not cleared data governance review will sit on a shelf. In financial services, the compliance review cycle for sending client financial data to an external AI provider typically takes 8–12 weeks. If this constraint is not surfaced during scoping, the team will discover it at deployment — after all the build and evaluation work is complete. Start the compliance conversations early. The [Compliance Readiness](../stages/03-scope.md#compliance-readiness-start-early) section of the methodology maps each compliance artifact to the framework stage that produces it, so you can give your compliance team a timeline rather than a surprise.

## Annotations

!!! note "Why Steps 1-4 are fully automated"
    These steps are pure data retrieval. There is no judgement involved -- you are pulling records from systems using account IDs and date ranges. If the API call succeeds, you have the data. If it fails, you have an error to handle. This is the most straightforward category of automation: no ambiguity, no interpretation, no risk of the agent making a "wrong" call.

!!! note "Why Step 5 (comms sentiment) is Human-in-the-Loop"
    Communication channels are messy and vary by account. Some customers live in Slack; others are email-only or primarily engage through scheduled calls. A single Slack thread might contain sarcasm and genuine complaints side by side; an email chain might bury a critical concern in a polite sign-off; a call summary might flag "pricing concerns" that were actually resolved live. NPS and relationship survey scores — where available — provide a structured quantitative anchor that cuts through this ambiguity: a detractor NPS from an executive sponsor is often the earliest warning of churn risk, well before usage metrics decline. But survey data has its own limitations (low response rates, stale scores, end-user responses that do not reflect sponsor sentiment), so the CSM still validates the composite picture. The agent retrieves from all configured sources and proposes a sentiment reading, but the CSM knows the relationship context — which channels carry the real signal for *this* account, whether "we need to talk about the roadmap" is routine or a veiled threat, and how to weight conflicting sentiment across channels. The more diverse the input sources, the more important this human validation becomes: cross-channel triangulation surfaces nuance that no single source reveals, but it also surfaces contradictions that require human judgement to resolve.

!!! note "Why Steps 6-7 split between AUTOMATE and HIL"
    The quantitative parts of ticket and usage analysis (calculating trends, comparing against SLA, computing adoption rates) are fully automatable. But interpretation of edge cases requires context the agent does not have. For example: a spike in ticket volume might look alarming, but the CSM knows it was caused by a planned migration. The split boundary lets the agent handle the math while the human handles the meaning.

!!! note "Why Step 8b exists separately from Step 8"
    Step 8 produces a diagnosis — what is going well, what is at risk. Step 8b produces a prescription — what to do about it. These are different cognitive tasks, and separating them prevents the common failure mode where risk identification drifts into vague recommendations like "monitor usage trends" or "keep an eye on sentiment." By making action-item generation its own step with explicit constraints (concrete verb, named owner, deadline, success criterion), the agent is forced to translate analysis into specifics. The AUTOMATE (draft) / HIL (validation) boundary is deliberate: the agent is good at mechanically applying urgency rules (Red + 60-day renewal = act this week) and drafting action language, but the CSM needs to validate that the proposed owner is correct, the deadline is realistic given their calendar, and the action is politically appropriate for the account relationship. The 5-7 item cap prevents action-plan bloat — a list of 15 actions is a list of zero actions.

!!! note "Traceability: Decision Logic → Stage 5 config"
    The Decision Logic entries for Steps 6, 7, and 9 are the source material for `config/scoring_rubric.yaml` in [Stage 5](build.md). The SLA targets by severity, tier-specific adoption benchmarks, and Green/Amber/Red criteria captured here translate directly into the externalised config that the agent loads at runtime. If you trace the numbers forward, you will find them unchanged in the Build stage — that is the point. Capture the criteria once in Stage 3; externalise them as config in Stage 5.

!!! note "Why Step 12 is fully MANUAL"
    Distribution is the one step where an error is irreversible -- once the report is sent, it is sent. The CSM needs to make final judgement calls about tone, completeness, and whether there is anything politically sensitive that needs rewording or omitting. This step also involves choosing the distribution list, which may vary by account. Keeping this manual is not a limitation; it is a deliberate safety boundary.

!!! note "Why Step 12b closes the operational loop"
    Without Step 12b, the agent produces a report but the CS platform's health score remains stale. The CSM now has two sources of truth: the report they just sent says Amber, but their platform dashboard still shows Green from last quarter. This is not a cosmetic problem — automated playbooks, manager dashboards, and renewal forecasting all read from the platform's health score field, not from a PDF attachment in someone's inbox. Step 12b writes the approved score back into the system of record immediately after distribution, so the platform reflects reality. The write-back is gated on Step 12 completion — the agent never updates the system of record with an unapproved score. For organisations that require explicit confirmation before any platform write, configure this step as HIL; for organisations that trust the Step 12 approval as sufficient authorisation, configure it as AUTOMATE. Either way, the key constraint is sequencing: Step 12b executes only after the CSM has reviewed, edited, and distributed the report.

!!! note "Relationship to existing CS platform health scores"
    If your organisation already has a platform-generated health score (Gainsight, Totango, ChurnZero, etc.), the agent does not replace it. Platform scores are typically rule-based aggregations of structured data — login frequency, support ticket counts, NPS responses — and they are your system of record. The agent's value is in the dimensions your platform cannot cover: qualitative sentiment from comms across channels (Slack, email, call recordings), narrative risk context from unstructured sources, and cross-source pattern synthesis that rule engines miss. Treat the agent's output as an enrichment layer. The workflow makes this concrete: Step 1b retrieves the platform's current health score alongside CRM data, and Step 9's decision logic explicitly compares the agent's assessment against it. Where the two diverge, the agent generates a reconciliation note explaining what evidence drove the difference — this is the "so what" for CSMs who already have automated scoring. A platform score of Green and an agent score of Amber, backed by declining Slack sentiment the platform cannot see, is immediately actionable. Divergence is signal, not error — it tells the CSM where structured metrics and qualitative reality do not match. If your org does not use a CS platform, Step 1b returns null and reconciliation is skipped; the rest of the workflow runs unchanged.

!!! note "Why account tier matters at the scope level, not just at scoring"
    The step-by-step workflow already contains tier-specific *thresholds* (Step 7's DAU/MAU benchmarks differ by enterprise, mid-market, and SMB). But thresholds within a step are different from skipping steps entirely or suppressing HIL checkpoints. A CSM managing 80 scaled accounts cannot afford a human pause at Step 5 for every one of them — but a CSM managing 12 strategic accounts absolutely needs it. The tier profile in the scoring rubric controls the *shape* of the agent's workflow, not just the numbers inside it. This is a structural decision that belongs in the scope document because it changes what the agent builds: a tiered workflow requires the agent to follow different paths for different account types. If you skip this constraint, you will build a one-size-fits-all agent and discover during pilot that your scaled-book CSMs abandon it because the review cycle is too heavy.

!!! tip "The boundary should err toward more human involvement in early versions"
    You can always push the boundary outward as trust builds. If after three quarters of use, the CSM finds they never change the agent's sentiment analysis, that HIL checkpoint can become fully automated. But you cannot easily *add* a checkpoint after users start relying on a fully automated step. Start conservative.
