What is this framework
and why should I care?

You are not about to learn how to code. You are about to learn how to think clearly about your own work—and reclaim the hours you lose to repetitive tasks every week.

⏲ ~20 minutes
📚 No technical skills required
☑ Ends with a self-check
Section 1 5 min read

You already know the problem

Think about last week. You probably pulled data from three or four systems, copied numbers into a spreadsheet or a slide deck, wrote up the same kind of summary you wrote last month, and sent it out. Same pattern, different data. Same steps, different quarter.

Now think about how much of that was genuinely hard. Not tedious—hard. The part that required your expertise, your knowledge of the customer, your judgement. If you are being honest, the hard part was probably 20% of the total time. The rest was mechanical.

This framework helps you figure out which of those workflows an AI agent could handle—and which parts you should keep doing yourself.

It is not a tool. It is not a product you are being asked to adopt. It is a way of thinking: a structured method for looking at your own work, identifying what is repeatable, and designing a process where an AI agent handles the repetitive parts while you handle the parts that actually need you.

Six stages. Three are yours.

The framework has six stages. They go from understanding your work all the way through to a working, tested agent. But here is the key thing: you own the first three stages. No code, no technical skills, no engineering background. Just clear thinking about your own work.

Stages 1–3 are about understanding your work. Stages 4–6 are about building and testing. With a low-code agent builder, you can do all six stages yourself. With a code-first framework, you hand off after Stage 3 to an engineer with a clear scope document.

By the end of this learning pathway, you will be able to complete Stages 1 through 3 for any workflow you own. That is a real, valuable skill—not just for this framework, but for any time someone asks you to explain how your work actually works.

Section 2 5 min read

What is an agentic workflow, actually?

Strip away the buzzwords. An agent is a program that follows a set of steps you define. That is it. It is not sentient. It is not creative. It is a very competent, very fast assistant that does exactly what you tell it to do, in the order you tell it to do it.

Here is what it can do:

  • 1. Pull data from the systems you use—your CRM, support platform, usage analytics, whatever you point it at.
  • 2. Analyse it—calculate trends, compare to benchmarks, identify patterns.
  • 3. Draft outputs—write a first version of the report, the summary, the health assessment.
  • 4. Pause for your review. Nothing goes out without your say-so.

That fourth step is non-negotiable. Every agentic workflow built with this framework has human-in-the-loop checkpoints. The agent proposes. You approve, edit, or reject. Then the agent continues—or stops.

Think of it as a shared notepad

🗒
The shared notepad metaphor

Imagine a notepad that sits between you and the agent. The agent writes what it finds and what it drafts on the notepad. You read it, mark it up, and write your own notes. The agent picks up where you left off. Neither of you loses track, because everything is on the notepad.

In technical terms, this notepad is called state—a running record of everything the agent has done and everything you have added. But you do not need to think about it technically. Just know that the agent remembers what it has done, what you changed, and what comes next.

Key concept

An agentic workflow is not a chatbot. You are not typing questions into a text box and hoping for useful answers. You are defining a repeatable process once, and the agent executes it the same way every time—pulling real data from real systems, producing a real output, and waiting for your real approval before anything goes anywhere.

Section 3 10 min read

What changes and what does not

Let us address the thing you might be thinking but have not said out loud yet.

“Will this replace me?”

No. And not in the vague, reassuring way people usually say that. Here is the specific, concrete reason: the agent cannot do the part of your job that actually matters.

An agent can pull data from your CRM. It cannot tell you that the champion at Acme Corp just got promoted and this changes the entire renewal conversation. An agent can calculate a health score. It cannot tell you that the score feels wrong because you were on a call last week and heard something in the customer's tone. An agent can draft a risk summary. It cannot decide whether to escalate to your VP now or wait until after the quarterly review.

That is your job. That has always been your job. The problem is that right now, you spend most of your time on the part that is not your job—the data gathering, the formatting, the first-draft writing—and too little time on the part that is.

That is not a hypothetical. That is the kind of time saving this framework is designed to deliver. And the report is often better—because you spent your time on the thinking instead of on the data wrangling.

Who does what

Here is the clear split. No ambiguity.

The agent handles
  • Data gathering from multiple systems
  • Calculations, trend analysis, comparisons
  • First-draft writing from templates and data
  • Formatting and structure
  • Routine data transformations
You handle
  • Judgement calls and prioritisation
  • Relationship context and nuance
  • Final review and sign-off
  • Distribution and communication
  • Escalation decisions

The agent does not attend your meetings, send your emails, or talk to your customers. It does the preparation work so you can show up to those conversations better prepared and with more time to think.

Your value shifts, it does not shrink

Right now, a significant portion of your week is spent on work that does not require your specific expertise. You do it because it needs to get done and there is nobody else to do it. An agent gives you that "somebody else." Not a person you need to manage or train—a process that runs the same way every time and never forgets a step.

The shift

Your value moves from doing the repetitive work to directing the work and applying judgement where it matters. The CSM who reviews and improves an agent-drafted health report in 30 minutes delivers more value than the CSM who spent 5 hours building it from scratch—because the thinking time was spent on insight, not on data entry.

Consider what you could do with the extra hours. More strategic conversations with customers. Deeper preparation for renewals. Proactive outreach instead of reactive firefighting. The time comes back to you, and you get to decide how to spend it.

Section 4 2 min

Quick self-check

Before you move on, take two minutes to think about your own work. You do not need to write anything down. Just let these questions sit for a moment. They will set you up for Lesson 2.

1

Think of a workflow you did last week that took more than an hour and followed roughly the same steps as last time you did it. What was it?

You do not need to name it formally. Just picture it—the tabs you opened, the systems you logged into, the document you produced at the end.

2

If you could have someone pull all the data for you and draft the first version, how much time would that save?

Be honest. Not the total time for the task—just the portion that is gathering and assembling, not thinking and judging.

3

What part of that workflow requires your specific judgement—something that no one else on your team, and certainly no program, could provide?

This is the part you keep. This is the part the agent will never touch.

4

If you got those hours back every week, what would you do with them?

Not more of the same work. What would you do that you currently do not have time for?

If you can picture that workflow and feel the weight of its repetitiveness, you are ready for Lesson 2. That is where you will learn how to break your work down systematically—not to justify automation, but to understand what you actually do each week.
