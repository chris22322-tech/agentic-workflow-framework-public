Lesson 2 Stage 1: Decompose ~30 minutes

Finding Your Workflows

Before you can automate anything, you need to know what you actually do. This lesson gives you a systematic method to surface the workflows you run on autopilot and evaluate which ones are worth automating.

Section 1 · 5 min

The Workflow Definition Test

Not everything you do is a workflow. Before building an inventory, you need a precise filter. A workflow must have exactly three properties:

A Trigger
Something that starts it
Repeatable Steps
A sequence you follow
A Concrete Output
Something it produces

Key distinction: If any of the three properties is missing, what you have is a responsibility (too broad) or a task (too narrow) — not a workflow.

Section 2 · 10 min

The Four-Angle Method

Most workflows live below conscious thought — you do them on autopilot. The trick to surfacing them is to approach your work from four different angles. Each angle catches workflows the others miss.

Time-Based

"What do I do every Monday? Every month-end? Every quarter?"

  • Weekly check-in preparation (every Monday)
  • Monthly account health review (first of month)
  • Quarterly Business Review deck assembly (end of quarter)

Trigger-Based

"What do I do when a ticket escalates? When a renewal is 90 days out?"

  • Escalation response package (when a P1 ticket is filed)
  • Renewal risk assessment (90 days before renewal)
  • New customer onboarding sequence (when contract is signed)

Output-Based

"What reports, documents, or summaries do I produce regularly?"

  • Client health scorecard (updated weekly)
  • Stakeholder update email with usage summary
  • Internal handoff brief when transferring an account

System-Based

"Why do I open Salesforce? What do I do in Jira? Why am I in Slack?"

  • Salesforce: Logging activity notes after every client call
  • Jira/Zendesk: Compiling ticket digest before a check-in
  • Slack: Gathering internal context from product and engineering channels

The point is to surface workflows you do on autopilot. If you sat down and asked only "What are my workflows?", you would miss half of them. The four angles force you to approach the question from different directions, catching the ones that are invisible because they are habitual.

Section 4 · 5 min

Scoring Automation Potential

Not every workflow should be automated. Look for four signals that indicate whether a workflow is a good candidate. For each signal, score the workflow High, Medium, or Low.

1

Structural Repeatability

"Weekly check-in prep follows the same pattern every time."
The steps are nearly identical across clients and weeks. This means the workflow has a stable structure that automation can replicate.

Strong
2

Determinism

"Deciding whether to escalate requires reading the room."
If a step depends on judgment, intuition, or context that is hard to encode, the workflow scores low on determinism. Automation struggles here.

Weak
3

Data Availability

"Usage metrics are in a dashboard with an API."
If the data you need is programmatically accessible (APIs, databases, structured exports), automation can pull it. If it is in someone's head or buried in email threads, it cannot.

Strong
4

Error Recoverability

"The summary is reviewed before the meeting."
If there is a natural review step where errors can be caught and corrected before they matter, the workflow is safer to automate. The human stays in the loop where it counts.

Strong

How to Score

Go back to your inventory table. For each workflow, assess the four signals and assign an overall automation potential:

High — 3–4 strong signals
Medium — 2 strong signals
Low — 0–1 strong signals

Remember: A "Low" score does not mean the workflow is unimportant. It means automation is not the right lever for improving it. Some workflows are better improved through process redesign or better tooling.

Section 5 · Reflection

Pause and Reflect

Before moving on, sit with your inventory for a moment. Three questions:

Question 01

"Look at your inventory. Which workflow scored highest for automation potential? Does that surprise you?"

Question 02

"Which workflow did you want to score highest? Is it the same one?"

Question 03

"If they are different, why? What made you want to automate the other one — frustration? Time cost? Visibility?"

If your highest-scoring workflow is not the one you most wanted to automate, the framework just saved you from automating the wrong thing. Desire to automate often tracks with frustration, not feasibility. The inventory and scoring process separates the two — and that distinction is exactly where failed automation projects begin.
