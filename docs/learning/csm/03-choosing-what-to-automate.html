Choosing What to Automate First

Your instinct says "automate the most painful thing." The framework says "automate the thing most likely to succeed." Here's how to tell the difference -- and why it matters.

3 / 8
1

Why Not Just Pick the Most Painful One?

5 min

You have your workflow inventory from Lesson 2. You know which processes eat your time, which ones you dread, and which ones feel like they should be automated. The temptation is obvious:

"I hate doing renewal prep. It takes hours. That's what I should automate first."

This instinct is completely natural -- and frequently wrong. Here's why: painful does not mean automatable. The workflow you hate most is often the most complex, highest-risk, or hardest to get data for. It's painful because it's hard, and hard things make terrible first automation projects.

Framework Principle
"Your first agent build should succeed. Success compounds. Failure discourages."

A successful first agent builds confidence -- yours, your manager's, your team's. It creates momentum for the second and third agents. A failed first attempt does the opposite: it breeds skepticism, consumes political capital, and makes "maybe we shouldn't automate things" the default stance for months.

The framework replaces gut feel with evidence. Here's what that looks like:

Gut Feel
"I'll automate QBR prep because it takes forever."
Score reveals: Feasibility 2 -- data scattered across 6 systems, 3 without APIs. Complexity 2 -- dozens of conditional paths depending on account tier, product mix, and stakeholder preferences. The agent would take months to build and still need constant babysitting.
Framework
"Weekly check-in prep scores 4.3 -- high feasibility, low risk."
Feasibility 5 -- data lives in 2 systems, both with APIs. Complexity 4 -- straightforward sequence with one branch point. And the pattern transfers directly to 3 other workflows. Ship it in weeks, not months.

The gut-feel choice wasn't wrong about the pain. It was wrong about the starting point. You can still automate QBR prep -- after you've built the muscle, proven the concept, and earned the trust.

2

The Six Scoring Dimensions

7 min

Every candidate workflow is scored on six dimensions, each rated 1 to 5. But not all dimensions carry equal weight -- Impact and Feasibility matter more than Learning Value because a high-impact, feasible project that teaches you nothing is still worth building, while a fascinating but infeasible one isn't.

Dimension Weight Score 1 (Low) Score 5 (High)
Impact
"How many hours per month does this eat?"
 30%
1 = Saves <30 min/month. Runs rarely. Affects only you.
5 = Saves 8+ hrs/month. Runs daily or weekly. Scales across the team.
Feasibility
"Can the agent actually GET the data it needs?"
 25%
1 = Data locked in PDFs, emails, or systems with no API. Manual copy-paste required.
5 = All data available via APIs or structured exports. Clean, reliable, well-documented.
Risk Tolerance
"What's the blast radius if the agent gets it wrong?"
 20%
1 = Error goes directly to a customer or executive. Damages trust, revenue, or compliance.
5 = Error caught internally before anyone sees it. Easy to fix. No downstream consequences.
Complexity
"How many decision points and branches?"
 10%
1 = 10+ decision points. Deeply nested conditionals. Outcome depends on nuance and context.
5 = Linear or near-linear flow. 0-2 decision points with clear criteria.
Learning Value
"Will building this teach patterns I can reuse?"
 5%
1 = One-off workflow. Unique structure. Patterns don't transfer to anything else.
5 = Core pattern (data gather → analyse → summarise) reused by 3+ other workflows.
Organisational Readiness
"Is my team ready for this change?"
 10%
1 = Team is skeptical of automation. Manager hasn't bought in. Process owners feel threatened.
5 = Team is asking for this. Manager is championing it. Clear executive sponsorship.
Why these weights?

Impact and Feasibility together carry 55% of the score. This ensures you never pick something that's easy but pointless (high Feasibility, low Impact) or valuable but impossible (high Impact, low Feasibility). The scoring forces you to find the intersection.

Each dimension is scored 1 to 5. The weighted composite is: (Impact × 0.30) + (Feasibility × 0.25) + (Risk Tolerance × 0.20) + (Complexity × 0.10) + (Learning Value × 0.05) + (Org Readiness × 0.10)

3

The Disqualifiers

3 min

Before you score, check these. If any of the following apply, skip this workflow -- no matter how high it might score. These are structural reasons why automation would destroy value rather than create it.

The value IS the relationship

The workflow's value comes from the relationship built during the process itself. Automating it removes the thing that makes it valuable.

Stakeholders would react negatively

Key stakeholders -- customers, executives, partners -- would react negatively to knowing an agent was involved in producing the output.

Data is locked behind inaccessible systems

The data sources are locked behind systems with no digital access -- no API, no export, no structured way to extract what the agent needs.

The workflow changes shape every time

The workflow changes significantly each time it runs. There is no stable, repeatable pattern for an agent to follow.

CSM Example -- Disqualified

EBR facilitation scores high on Impact -- it consumes significant time and the output is high-visibility. But the preparation IS the relationship-building. The conversations you have while gathering input, the political dynamics you navigate when choosing what to present, the trust you build by showing you understand the account deeply -- these are the point. An agent that gathered and assembled the data would remove the very activity that makes EBRs valuable. The framework disqualifies it.

Note: a disqualified workflow might contain sub-workflows that ARE automatable. The EBR's data-gathering step might be a great agent candidate -- even if the facilitation itself is not. You explored sub-workflows in Lesson 2.

Key Insight

If the winner surprises you, the framework is working. The whole point of structured scoring is to override the cognitive biases that pull you toward the most painful, most exciting, or most politically visible workflow -- none of which correlate with "most likely to succeed as a first agent."

5

Reflection

What You Now Have

A defensible, evidence-based answer to "what should we automate first?" -- not a hunch, not a guess, not "whatever seemed exciting in the moment." You have a scored comparison across six dimensions with explicit weights and clear reasoning.

You can present this scoring to your manager. The numbers speak. When someone asks "why this workflow and not that one?" you can point to the scores, explain the weights, and show that your recommendation accounts for impact, feasibility, risk, complexity, learning value, and organisational readiness. That's a business case, not a request.
