Mapping Your Workflow Step by Step

The quality bar for writing a workflow is simple: could someone who has never done this work follow your instructions without asking a single question? If not, an agent definitely cannot.

4 / 8
1

The New Hire Test

5 min
The Quality Bar

Write your workflow as if you are explaining it to someone who has never done it and cannot ask follow-up questions.

This is not a hypothetical standard. It is the actual test your workflow documentation needs to pass. If a brand-new hire, sitting alone on their first day, could not follow your instructions end-to-end without picking up the phone or sending a Slack message, then your instructions are not specific enough.

And if a new hire cannot follow them, an agent definitely cannot.

An agent is more literal than the most literal new hire you have ever met. It does not fill in gaps with common sense. It does not "know what you probably meant." It does exactly what you wrote. Nothing more, nothing less.

Here is what this looks like in practice:

Vague

"Review the account's health"

Specific

"Pull the account record from Salesforce (ARR, contract dates, tier). Pull support tickets from Zendesk for this quarter (filter by account ID, date range). Compare ticket volume and severity against last quarter. Flag if volume is up >20% or average severity shifted from Low to Medium+."

The vague version assumes the reader knows what "health" means, which systems to check, which fields matter, and what "good" looks like. A new hire reading that instruction would immediately have five questions. An agent reading it would produce garbage.

The specific version tells you exactly where to go, exactly what to pull, exactly what to compare, and exactly when to raise a flag. No ambiguity. No interpretation required.

Why this matters

The gap between vague and specific is not about writing skill. It is about whether you have ever had to articulate what you actually do. Most CSMs have done these tasks so many times that the steps are muscle memory. This lesson is about converting muscle memory into explicit instructions.

2

The Step Format

5 min

Every step in your workflow map needs five components. Not three, not "whatever feels right." Five. If any of these are missing, the step is incomplete and an agent will not be able to execute it reliably.

  • Action -- what is being done (verb + object)
  • Input -- what data or information goes in
  • Output -- what is produced when the step is complete
  • Decision Logic -- any rules, thresholds, or judgement calls
  • Time -- roughly how long the step takes you today

Here is one step from a CSM quarterly health report, written in full:

Step 3: Assess Support Ticket Health
Action
Compare this quarter's support ticket volume and severity against last quarter for the account
Input
Zendesk tickets filtered by account ID and date range (current quarter vs. previous quarter). Fields: ticket count, severity (Low / Medium / High / Critical)
Output
A summary line stating ticket trend (improving / stable / degrading) and a flag if the account needs attention
Decision Logic
Flag as "degrading" if: ticket volume up >20% quarter-over-quarter OR average severity shifted from Low to Medium+. Flag as "improving" if: volume down >15% AND no severity increase.
Time
~12 minutes per account (manual Zendesk query, data comparison, summary writing)

Now here is the same step written badly:

Step 3: Check tickets (incomplete)
Action
"Check the tickets"
Input
"Zendesk"
Output
"Whether tickets look okay"
Decision Logic
"Use your judgement"
Time
"A few minutes"

The bad version is what most people write on their first attempt. It feels complete because you know what it means. But you are not the audience. A new hire reading "check the tickets" has no idea which tickets, in which system, for which time period, or what "okay" means. Neither does an agent.

3

Decision Logic -- The Hardest Part

10 min

Of the five components in each step, decision logic is the one that trips people up. Not because it is conceptually hard, but because most of us have never had to articulate the rules we use every day. We just... know.

The trick is recognising that not all decisions are the same. There are three types, and they require very different treatment:

1
No Decision
Pure data retrieval or transformation. There is nothing to decide -- you are just pulling or moving data.
"Pull CRM data for account X"
No logic needed
2
Criteria-Based
Rules you can write down. If X, then Y. Thresholds, comparisons, lookups against known values.
"Flag if CSAT < 4.0"
Write the rule explicitly
3
Contextual Judgement
You weigh multiple factors, use relationship context, or read between the lines. No formula captures it.
"Is the customer frustrated?"
Human stays in the loop

The distinction between type 2 and type 3 is where most of the work happens. Many things that feel like contextual judgement turn out to be criteria-based once you force yourself to write down the rules. And the things that truly are contextual judgement -- those are exactly where your human expertise stays essential.

The Specificity Test

Could someone write a working instruction from this entry alone?

If the answer is no, your criteria-based logic is not specific enough yet. Keep pushing until someone with no context could implement it.

Apply this test to every criteria-based decision in your workflow. Here are three before-and-after examples:

X

"Assess ticket health"

✓

Compare this quarter's ticket count and average severity against last quarter. Flag as degrading if volume up >20% OR average severity shifted from Low to Medium+.

Criteria-based
X

"Check product engagement"

✓

Calculate DAU/MAU ratio. Compare against tier benchmark (Enterprise: 60%, Mid-market: 40%). Flag as at-risk if below benchmark by >15%.

Criteria-based
X

"Read the room on sentiment"

■

This stays as contextual judgement. You are reading tone, body language, and relationship history. No formula captures this.

Human-in-the-loop

Notice the pattern. The first two examples felt like judgement calls, but once forced to articulate the criteria, they turned out to be straightforward rules. The third one? That is genuinely contextual. There is no formula for "reading the room." That is where you, the human, stay in the loop.

The uncomfortable truth

The reason decision logic is the hardest part is not that it is complex. It is that it requires you to confront how much of your daily work is actually rule-based and how much is genuinely novel. Most people discover the split is about 70/30 -- far more rule-based than they expected.

5

Reflection

2 min

The Specificity Test Is Hard. That Is Normal.

If you are struggling to articulate your criteria, that is not a sign you are doing it wrong. It is a sign you have been making these decisions intuitively for years. You built up pattern recognition and heuristics that live in your head, not on paper. Writing them down is genuinely difficult.

The good news: once you write it down, you never have to do the repetitive part again. Every minute you spend getting the criteria right is a minute you will never spend executing those criteria manually. The agent handles the rules. You handle the judgement.

In Lesson 5, you will take these steps and draw the line: which ones the agent does, which ones need your review, and which ones stay entirely with you.
