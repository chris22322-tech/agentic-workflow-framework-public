What Happens Next -- Design, Build, Evaluate

Your scope document is done. Now you need to know what happens with it -- whether you are configuring the agent yourself or working with a technical partner. Two paths, same methodology, same outcome.

1

Two Paths from Here

5 min

Your scope document is a complete blueprint. What happens next depends on how you want to build: do it yourself with a no-code platform, or work with an engineer who writes code. Both paths start from the same artifact -- your scope document -- and both follow the same methodology.

From Scope Document to Working Agent
Your Scope Document
Path A -- No-Code
Low-code agent builder
You configure the agent yourself in a visual builder. No engineer needed.
Your scope steps become the agent's instructions
Your data inventory becomes your tool configuration
Your HIL checkpoints become approval rules
Your error paths become fallback instructions
Path B -- With Engineering
Code-first framework
You hand off your scope document to an engineer who builds it in code. You stay involved for design validation and evaluation.
Your scope steps become agent actions
Your data inventory becomes tool integrations
Your HIL checkpoints become pause-and-resume points
Your error paths become programmatic error handling
Key insight

Either way, YOUR scope document drives the design. The methodology is the same. The only difference is who translates the scope into a working agent -- you or an engineer.

2

What the Design Stage Looks Like

3 min

Design is where your scope document gets translated into something that can be built. What this looks like depends on your path:

Path A: You Build It
Low-code agent builder

You map your scope steps to the builder's tool types: your CRM becomes a tool connection, your support platform becomes another. You write the agent's instructions directly from your scope document -- the steps, decision logic, and error paths become the instructions the agent follows. Your HIL checkpoints become approval rules in those instructions.

Path B: Engineer Builds It
Code-first framework

The engineer translates your scope into an agent design. You review the design to verify: every scope step maps to an action, every HIL step has a pause checkpoint, and the data sources match your inventory. You attend a handoff meeting where you walk through the scope document together.

Regardless of path, the design stage produces the same thing: a clear plan for how the agent will work, reviewed by you (the domain expert) before anyone starts building.

Design validation checklist

Whether you are reviewing your own low-code builder configuration or an engineer's agent design, check these three things: (1) every scope step maps to something in the design, (2) every HIL checkpoint has a real pause-and-review mechanism, and (3) the data sources in the design match your data inventory exactly. If any of these are missing, fix it before building.

3

What the Build Stage Looks Like

2 min

Build is where the design becomes a working agent. Your role differs depending on the path:

Path A: You Build It
Hands-on Configuration

You configure tools in Agent Builder, connect your data sources, write and refine the system instruction, and test in the console. You iterate directly -- change the instruction, run a test, see the output, adjust. The feedback loop is immediate.

Path B: Engineer Builds It
Available for Questions

The engineer writes code. You are available for questions about decision logic, edge cases, and "how do you actually decide this in practice?" moments. You review the HIL checkpoint experience to make sure it matches how you actually want to review and approve agent output.

The build stage typically takes 1-2 weeks for a first agent. Path A (no-code) is often faster for simpler workflows because you can iterate without waiting on an engineer's schedule. Path B is better for complex workflows with many data integrations or custom logic.

4

Evaluation -- This Is Where You Come Back

5 min

Regardless of who built the agent, you evaluate it. You are the domain expert. You know what a good health report looks like. You know when a risk rating is wrong. You know when a narrative misses the point. No engineer or platform can evaluate this for you.

Here is what evaluation looks like: run the agent on 5 test accounts and compare the output to what you would have produced manually. Pick accounts that represent different scenarios:

1
Test Case
Healthy account, no issues
2
Test Case
At-risk account, declining usage
3
Test Case
New customer, limited data
4
Test Case
Enterprise account, complex stakeholder map
5
Test Case
Renewal in 30 days, mixed signals

For each test account, you evaluate three dimensions:

  • Correctness: "Is this health score right? Did it calculate the trends correctly? Are the data points accurate?"
  • Completeness: "Did it miss the Slack sentiment? Did it forget the support escalation from last week? Is anything important absent?"
  • Quality: "Would I send this to my VP as-is? Does the narrative flow? Is the tone appropriate for this account?"
Set your expectations

The agent's first output will be a B-. Your feedback makes it an A-. That is the iteration cycle. You are not looking for perfection on the first run -- you are looking for a foundation that is worth refining.

Each round of feedback tightens the agent. You refine the system instruction, adjust scoring criteria, add missing context. After 2-3 iterations, the agent's output should require only light editing -- the kind of review that takes 15 minutes instead of 90 minutes of creation from scratch.

5

After Graduation

3 min

Once your agent passes evaluation, you do not flip a switch and walk away. There is a deliberate ramp-up:

Phase 1: Shadow Mode (2-4 cycles)
Run the agent alongside your manual process

Do the work yourself as you normally would. Also run the agent on the same accounts. Compare outputs side by side. This builds your confidence in the agent's quality and catches edge cases before you depend on it.

Phase 2: Switch Over
When you are editing less than 15 minutes per report, switch

The signal is clear: if you are spending more time editing the agent's output than it saves you, stay in shadow mode. If you are making minor tweaks and moving on, you are ready to let the agent do the heavy lifting.

Phase 3: Expand
Pick the next workflow from your Stage 1 inventory

Go back to the workflow inventory you built in Lesson 2. Pick the next highest-scoring workflow. Run it through the same methodology. The first agent is the hardest. The second one takes half the time because you already understand the process.

Looking Ahead

You now understand the full lifecycle: scope, design, build, evaluate, deploy. You know what your role is at every stage, whether you are building the agent yourself or working with a technical partner.

In Lesson 8 -- the final lesson -- you will put it all together into a concrete action plan with specific steps and timeframes. You will leave the pathway with a clear list of what to do next.
