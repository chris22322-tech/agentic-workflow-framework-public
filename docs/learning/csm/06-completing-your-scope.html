What's Left?

3 min

Let us take stock of where you are. Over the past three lessons, you have built the core of your scope document:

  • Workflow steps -- the sequential actions that make up your workflow (Lesson 4)
  • Decision logic -- the if/then rules that govern each step (Lesson 4)
  • Boundary tags -- AUTOMATE, HIL, or MANUAL for every step (Lesson 5)

That is the skeleton. Now you need the four remaining pieces to make it a complete, actionable scope document:

  • Error paths -- what happens when something goes wrong at each step
  • Data inventory -- where your data lives and how you access it today
  • Constraints and assumptions -- what must be true for the workflow to work
  • Future-state summary -- the before/after view that makes the change tangible
Why this matters

These four pieces are the difference between a scope document that describes your workflow and one that can actually be built from. Whether you configure the agent yourself on a low-code builder or hand this to an engineer, these details prevent the "wait, what about..." conversations that derail projects.

2

Error Paths -- What Happens When Things Go Wrong

7 min

For every step in your workflow, ask three questions: What if the data is not there? What if it is wrong? What if the human rejects the output? These are your error paths, and they are the most commonly overlooked part of scope documents.

Every failure falls into one of four categories. Here is each one, with a CSM example you will recognise:

Data Unavailable
The system is down or the data does not exist

"Your CRM is down during the health review window. The agent cannot pull ARR or contract dates."

Agent flags the gap, continues with available data, and notes what is missing in the output for you to fill in manually.
Data Malformed
The data is there but it is wrong or in the wrong format

"CSAT scores are showing as text strings instead of numbers, or dates are in European format when the agent expects US format."

Agent validates data types before processing. If validation fails, it flags the specific field and skips that calculation rather than producing a wrong number.
Output Rejected
The agent produces output that is factually wrong

"You review the draft health report and the risk rating says 'green' for an account you know is struggling. The agent weighted usage metrics too heavily and missed the support escalation."

At the HIL checkpoint, you correct the rating. The agent logs the correction so the scoring criteria can be refined for next time.
Human Rejects
The output is technically correct but misses the point

"The exec summary is factually accurate but leads with product adoption metrics when you know the VP only cares about ROI. It misses the point entirely."

You rewrite the framing at the HIL checkpoint. Over time, you add context to the agent's instructions about audience priorities so it learns what to lead with.
Design principle

The agent should degrade gracefully -- flag gaps, do not crash. A partial output with clear annotations of what is missing is always better than no output at all.

Here is what an error path row looks like in your scope document. You will add one of these for each step that has an AUTOMATE or HIL tag:

Step Failure Mode Agent Behaviour Your Action
Pull support ticket data
Support platform API returns empty (maintenance window or auth expired)
Agent marks ticket section as "[DATA UNAVAILABLE -- support platform unreachable]" and continues to next step
You notice the flag during HIL review, pull the data manually, and paste it in
Why this matters for building

When you configure the agent yourself on a low-code builder, these error paths become part of the agent's instructions -- you tell it exactly how to handle each failure. If you hand this to an engineer, they implement these as programmatic error handling in the agent's code. Either way, the error paths prevent the agent from silently producing garbage.

Data Inventory -- Where Does Your Data Live?

5 min

For each system you use in this workflow, you need three things: the system name, what data you pull from it, and how you access it today. You do not need to know API details -- that is for your IT team or the build stage. You just need to catalogue where data lives and how you currently get to it.

Here is a typical CSM data inventory:

System Data You Pull How You Access It Today
CRM (Salesforce)
ARR, contract dates, tier, renewal date, account owner, opportunity history
Salesforce dashboard + manual report export
Support Platform (Zendesk)
Open tickets, severity, resolution time, CSAT per ticket, escalation history
Zendesk reports tab, sometimes CSV export
Usage Analytics (Pendo)
DAU/MAU, feature adoption %, login frequency, time-in-app
Pendo dashboard, weekly email digest
Slack
Customer channel activity, sentiment signals, recent questions or complaints
Read channel manually, keyword search
CS Platform (Gainsight)
Health score, CTAs, timeline entries, success plan progress
Gainsight cockpit view
NPS Tool (Delighted)
NPS score, response rate, verbatim comments, trend over time
Monthly email report + Delighted dashboard
Practical tip

If you can export a CSV or see it in a dashboard, the data is accessible. You do not need to understand APIs -- just note how you access the data today. During the build stage, you or an engineer will figure out the best way to connect the agent. If you are building on a low-code platform, your data inventory becomes your tool configuration -- each system maps to one of the tool types the builder supports.

Your inventory does not need to be this detailed on the first pass. Start with the systems you know you use, and note what you pull from each one. You can always add detail later.

4

Constraints and Assumptions

3 min

What must be true for this workflow to work as designed? These are the preconditions and boundaries that your scope document assumes. Writing them down prevents surprises later.

Here are some common CSM constraints:

  • Single-account processing: The agent processes one account at a time. It does not batch-process your entire book of business in one run.
  • Stable scoring criteria: Your health scoring criteria (thresholds, weights, categories) do not change between runs. If they change, the agent's instructions need to be updated.
  • Channel existence: If the workflow reads from Slack channels, those channels must already exist and the agent must have access. It will not create channels.
  • Data freshness: The agent uses data as of the moment it runs. If your CRM data is updated weekly but you run the agent daily, some data will be stale.
  • No external communication: The agent never sends emails, Slack messages, or any communication to customers or external stakeholders. Distribution is always MANUAL.
Data sensitivity check

Ask yourself: Does any of the data in this workflow contain customer PII? Revenue figures under NDA? Contract terms with confidentiality clauses? If yes, note it as a constraint. This does not stop the project -- it just means you need to confirm with your security or legal team that the agent's data access is appropriate. Better to surface this now than during the build.

5

The Future-State Summary

5 min

The future-state summary is a before/after view of your workflow. It answers one question: what changes for you when this agent is running?

This is not a technical document. It is the artifact you share with your manager, your team, or anyone who needs to understand what you are building and why. It makes the change tangible.

CSM Quarterly Health Report -- Before vs After
Today (Manual)
You spend 45 minutes per account pulling data from 5 systems
You manually calculate trend lines and compare to last quarter
You write the health narrative from scratch every time
You format everything into your report template by hand
You review your own work because there is no second check
Total: ~90 minutes per account, every quarter
With Agent
Agent pulls data from all 5 systems in under 2 minutes
Agent calculates trends, flags anomalies, and compares to benchmarks
Agent drafts the health narrative using your template and scoring criteria
Agent formats everything into your report template automatically
You review the draft at one HIL checkpoint, edit tone and emphasis
Total: ~20 minutes per account (15 min review + 5 min polish)

Notice what the future-state summary communicates without any technical language: the time savings, the shift in your role (from data gatherer to quality reviewer), and the fact that you still own the final output. This is what makes stakeholders say "yes, let's do this."

Your Turn

Draft Your Future-State Summary

Think about the workflow you have been scoping. What does "before" look like? What does "after" look like? Write a few bullet points for each side. Focus on: time per task, your role in each step, and the quality of the output.

Your Scope Document Is Complete

Take a moment to appreciate what you have produced. This is the most detailed map of your own workflow you have ever created. It includes the steps, the decision logic, the automation boundary, the error paths, the data inventory, the constraints, and a clear before/after view of the future state.

This is a real artifact. Whether you configure the agent yourself on a low-code platform or hand this to an engineer building on a code-first framework, the scope document tells them -- or you -- exactly what to build. No ambiguity, no guessing, no "I'll figure it out as I go."
