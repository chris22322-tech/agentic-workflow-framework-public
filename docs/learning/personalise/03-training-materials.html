Section 1 3 min read

What Layer 2 produces

Layer 1 gave you the config—a structured description of your organisation, its roles, its systems, and its workflows. Layer 2 takes that config and turns it into learning materials that are specific to each role.

For every role defined in your org config, the AI generates a complete set of training materials:

👋
A role introduction

Grounded in THEIR responsibilities, not a generic overview. The learner sees their own job title and their actual context from the first paragraph.

🛠
Stage 1–3 walkthroughs

Using THEIR workflows as examples. When an Implementation Manager reads Stage 1, they see deployment workflows—not account health reviews.

📄
A complete worked example

Built from one of THEIR real workflows, taken end-to-end through the framework. Concrete, specific, and immediately applicable.

✏
Practice exercises

Tied to THEIR systems and data. Not hypotheticals—actual workflows they can decompose using the tools they already know.

🎯
An assessment rubric

Calibrated to THEIR context so managers can evaluate progress using criteria that make sense for the role.

Generic vs personalised: the difference matters

The generic framework teaches concepts well. But when someone reads a worked example about a role that is not theirs, they have to do extra cognitive work to translate. Personalised materials eliminate that translation step.

The same concept, two versions
Generic

“A CSM reviews account health by pulling data from the CRM, checking support ticket trends, and drafting a summary for the customer.”

➞
Personalised

“An Implementation Manager at Acme reviews client deployment status by pulling data from Jira, checking milestone completion in Confluence, and drafting a progress update for the project sponsor.”

The second version takes zero extra thought. The reader sees their own role, their own tools, their own deliverable. They are learning the framework without realising they are translating anything.

Section 2 4 min read

Running the prompts

Layer 2 uses three prompts. Each one takes your org config as input and produces a specific set of training outputs. You run them one at a time, review the results, and iterate if needed.

👤
Role Learning Path
personalise/prompts/learning/role-path.md
Generates a complete learning pathway for a single role. Includes the role introduction, stage walkthroughs, worked example, practice exercises, and assessment rubric—all grounded in that role's workflows and systems from your org config.
Produces: One complete learning module per role (run once per role in your config)
👥
Workshop Plan
personalise/prompts/learning/workshop.md
Generates a facilitator guide for a 2-hour hands-on workshop. Includes pre-work instructions, session plans with timing, group activities, and a take-home assignment. Designed to be run by a champion or team lead, not an external trainer.
Produces: A facilitator guide + participant handout + pre-work email template
🏆
Champion Programme
personalise/prompts/learning/champion-programme.md
Generates a champion enablement programme: selection criteria, training plan for champions, monthly office hours format, and an adoption dashboard template. Champions become your internal multipliers.
Produces: Champion selection guide + training plan + office hours agenda template

Tip: Start with one role. Run the role-path prompt, review the output, and refine your org config if the results feel off. Then generate for the other roles. Trying to do them all at once means you will not catch issues early.

How it works in practice

Feed your org-config.yaml to the prompt. The AI reads your role definitions, workflows, and systems, then generates materials that reference those specifics throughout. If your config says an Implementation Manager uses Jira and Confluence, every example in their learning path will use Jira and Confluence—not Salesforce and Google Sheets.

Section 3 3 min read

The workshop format

The workshop is a 2-hour hands-on session designed to get a team from "I have heard of this framework" to "I have completed Stage 1 for one of my own workflows." It is practical by design—no slides-only presentations.

2-hour workshop structure
15 min
Pre-work

Each participant lists 3 workflows they do regularly. No detail needed yet—just the names. This primes their thinking before the session starts.

30 min
Session 1: Group Stage 1 walkthrough

The facilitator takes one participant's role and walks through Stage 1 (Decompose) live with the group. Everyone sees the process in action before trying it themselves.

30 min
Session 2: Individual practice

Everyone completes their own workflow inventory. Using their pre-work list, each person works through Stage 1 for their own role. The facilitator circulates and helps.

30 min
Session 3: Pairs

Participants pair up to score and compare candidates. Each pair reviews the other's inventory, applies the selection criteria from Stage 2, and debates which workflows are the best candidates.

15 min
Session 4: Group discussion

Pairs share their selections with the group. The facilitator captures shared systems and common workflow patterns across roles—these become candidates for org-wide automation.

Takeaway: Every participant leaves with a completed Stage 1 inventory and a shortlisted automation candidate.

The pre-work is essential. Without it, participants spend the first 20 minutes of the session trying to think of workflows on the spot, which wastes group time. A short email three days before the workshop is enough.

The workshop prompt generates all of this for you: the facilitator guide, the participant handout, the pre-work email template, and the timing notes. You do not need to design it from scratch.

Section 4 2 min read

Sustaining adoption

Training without follow-through is a waste of everyone's time. The workshop gets people started, but sustained adoption requires lightweight structures that keep momentum going without becoming overhead.

🏆
Champions

1–2 per team, trained first. They run monthly office hours where teammates can bring questions, share progress, and troubleshoot. Low effort, high impact.

🤝
Buddy system

Pairs review each other's artifacts. When someone completes a workflow decomposition, their buddy checks it. Peer review catches gaps and builds shared understanding.

🔁
Spaced repetition

15-minute micro-exercises at weeks 1, 2, and 4 after the workshop. Short enough that nobody skips them, spaced enough that the concepts stick.

📊
Adoption tracker

Measure how many people completed each stage. Not as surveillance—as visibility. Teams that can see progress tend to maintain momentum.

The pattern

Train champions first. Champions run workshops. Workshops create buddies. Buddies sustain each other. The adoption tracker lets leadership see progress without micromanaging. Each layer reinforces the next.

“The goal is not 100% completion in week one. The goal is steady progress where each person has completed at least Stage 1 within a month—and has a buddy who has reviewed their work.”
