Step 1 5 min read
1

Fill in your Org Config

The org config file is a simple YAML file that tells the AI about your organisation, your team, and your goals. Think of it as a brief for a very capable new hire who is about to write you a personalised automation strategy.

There are five sections. None of them take more than a minute. The more honest and specific you are, the better the output will be.

🏢
Your Organisation

Company name, industry, and division — just the basics. This information appears throughout your personalised guide, so it reads like something written for your team rather than a generic template.

Examplecompany_name: Acme Corp industry: Financial Services division: Client Implementation
👥
Your Roles

List 2–5 roles on your team. For each one, write 2–3 sentences about what they do day-to-day. Not the HR job description — what they actually spend their hours doing.

Example Role DescriptionImplementation Manager: Manages client implementation projects from kickoff to go-live. Coordinates across engineering, product, and client teams. Owns delivery timelines and risk registers.
💡
Tip: Write it the way you would describe the role to a new hire, not the way HR wrote the job description. The AI uses these descriptions to identify which workflows belong to which role, so natural language works best.
🖥
Your Systems

List every system your team uses regularly. If you know whether it has an API, note that too. If you do not know, just list the system — the AI will figure out the integration story.

System Has API?
Jira Yes
Salesforce Yes
Shared Google Sheets Yes (Google Sheets API)
Slack Yes
💡
Tip: If you do not know about APIs, just list the systems. The AI will figure out the rest. Most popular business tools have APIs — the AI knows about them already.
🔒
Your Constraints

This is where you describe the guardrails your team operates within. Regulatory environment, data sensitivity levels, whether outputs go directly to clients, approval chains — all of it matters.

These constraints shape the guardrails section of your personalised guide. Be honest — the AI produces better output when it knows your real constraints rather than the idealised version.

Example Constraintsregulatory: FCA-regulated environment data_sensitivity: Client financial data, PII client_facing: Yes, reports go directly to clients approvals: All client deliverables require manager sign-off
🎯
Your Goals

What are you trying to achieve? Be specific. “Save time on weekly reporting” is far more useful than “implement AI.” The AI uses your goals to prioritise which workflows to recommend automating first.

Example Goals- Reduce time spent on weekly client status reports - Standardise onboarding documentation across the team - Get early warning when implementations fall behind schedule - Free up senior staff for strategic client conversations

That is the entire config. Five sections, each a few lines. If you can describe your team to a colleague, you can fill in this file.

Step 2 5 min read
2

Run the Prompt

Once your config file is ready, you have three ways to run Layer 1. Each produces the same output — a personalised Application Guide — but they differ in effort and output quality. Pick the one that matches your comfort level.

Easiest

Run it in your browser

No setup, no installation. Just copy and paste.

  1. Open Claude.ai or ChatGPT in your browser
  2. Paste the contents of your org-config.yaml file into the chat
  3. Paste the application guide prompt after it
  4. Wait approximately 10–15 minutes while the AI generates your guide
  5. Copy the output into a new document — that is your Application Guide
💡
Tip: For best results, paste both the config and the prompt in a single message. This gives the AI the full context in one shot.
Faster, better output

Run it from the command line

If you have Claude Code installed, the CLI gives the AI direct access to the framework documentation — which means richer context and better output.

claude -p "$(cat personalise/prompts/application-guide.md)" --max-turns 30

The AI reads the framework docs directly, understands the full structure, and writes a more comprehensive guide. Output is written to:

personalise/output/APPLICATION-GUIDE.md
💡
Tip: The --max-turns 30 flag gives the AI enough room to read through all the framework files before writing. Do not reduce it or the output may be truncated.
Zero-touch

Let GitHub Actions do it for you

If you have forked the framework repository, you can trigger Layer 1 automatically:

  1. Fork the repo and add your completed org-config.yaml
  2. Push your config to the repository
  3. The GitHub Action runs automatically on push
  4. Your personalised guide appears in the repo when the action completes

This approach is ideal if you plan to run Layer 1 for multiple teams or want to keep your configs and outputs version-controlled.

Whichever path you choose, the output is the same: a comprehensive, personalised Application Guide that maps the framework to your specific organisation.

Step 3 3 min read

What You Get

The Application Guide is a single document, typically 15–30 pages, structured to be immediately actionable. Here is what each section contains:

📜
Executive Summary

A one-page overview you can share with leadership. Covers the opportunity, recommended approach, and expected timeline.

📅
Rollout Plan

A phased timeline showing which workflows to automate first, second, and third — with dependencies mapped out.

📖
Role Playbooks

One playbook per role, with scored workflows showing automation potential, effort, and expected time savings.

⚡
Quick Wins

The top 5 workflows across all roles that offer the highest return for the lowest effort. Start here.

🔒
Data Sensitivity Classification

Every data source classified by sensitivity level, with handling recommendations for each tier.

🛡
Guardrails & Policies

Recommended policies for human-in-the-loop checkpoints, data access, and output review — shaped by your constraints.

⚠
Risk Register

Potential risks identified from your config, with mitigations and owners suggested for each.

🛠
Framework Enhancement Recommendations

Suggestions for extending the framework based on patterns unique to your organisation and industry.

Key Insight

The guide is not a generic report. Because it is built from your config, every recommendation references your actual roles, your actual systems, and your actual constraints. That is what makes it immediately usable rather than something you file away and forget.

Step 4 2 min read
4

What to Do With It

You have your Application Guide. Now what? Here are five concrete next steps, in order. You do not need to do all five today — but the first two should happen within a week while the context is fresh.

  1. Share the executive summary with your manager.
    It is written to be self-explanatory. You should not need to present it — just send it with a one-line note: “I ran our team through the automation framework. Here is what it recommends.”
  2. Share role playbooks with 1–2 people per role for an accuracy check.
    The AI does a good job, but the people doing the work know best. Ask them: “Does this match what you actually do? What did it miss?” Mark up the playbook with their feedback.
  3. Use the quick wins to pick your first workflow.
    Do not try to automate everything at once. The quick wins section ranks workflows by effort-to-value ratio. Pick the top one. That is your pilot.
  4. Schedule a team session to walk through the guide together.
    Thirty minutes is enough. Share the guide in advance and use the session to discuss priorities, answer questions, and build buy-in. People support what they help shape.
  5. Move to Layer 2 to generate training materials.
    Once the guide is validated, Layer 2 takes each workflow and produces step-by-step training content your team can actually use. That is the next page in this pathway.

The goal is not perfection — it is momentum. A good-enough guide that your team has seen and validated is worth infinitely more than a perfect guide sitting in a folder.

🏁
You have just completed the hardest part

Filling in the config and running the prompt takes less than an hour. Reviewing and sharing the output takes another hour. In under two hours, you have gone from “we should probably look into AI” to a concrete, personalised strategy. That is Layer 1.
