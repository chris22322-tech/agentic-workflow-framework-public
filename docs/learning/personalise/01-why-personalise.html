Section 1 5 min read

Why personalise at all?

The Agentic Workflow Framework is generic by design. It works for any knowledge worker at any company: a CSM at a SaaS startup, a compliance analyst at a bank, a programme manager at a consultancy. That generality is a feature—it is what makes the framework broadly useful.

But generic training has a well-known problem.

People read “A Customer Success Manager reviews account health quarterly” and think: “That is not my job.”

They are right. It is not their job. It is a proxy for their job, and proxies create friction. People have to mentally translate every example, every workflow, every system name from the generic version into their own reality. Some will do that translation. Most will not. They will skim the content, decide it is not relevant, and move on. Your investment in enablement walks out the door.

Personalisation fixes this. It replaces generic examples with your roles, your systems, and your workflows. The result is training material that reads as if it were written for your company specifically—because it was.

See the difference

Generic

“A Customer Success Manager reviews account health quarterly using CRM, support tickets, and usage data to identify at-risk accounts and produce a health report for the leadership team.”

Personalised

“An Implementation Manager at [Your Company] reviews client deployment progress weekly using Jira, Confluence, and the client's deployment tracking dashboard to flag blockers and produce a status update for the delivery lead.”

Same framework. Same methodology. But the second version speaks directly to someone who lives in that world. They do not have to translate. They recognise their workflow, their tools, their cadence. That recognition is the difference between training that sticks and training that gets forgotten by Thursday.

Section 2 5 min read

The three layers of personalisation

Personalisation is not a single step. It is three layers, each building on the last. You can stop after any layer—each one delivers standalone value—but they compound. Layer 1 feeds Layer 2. Layer 2 feeds Layer 3. By the end, your team has a fully branded, role-specific learning site that reads as if a training consultancy built it from scratch for your organisation.

1

Application Guide

~2 hours

Fill in a config file about your organisation—name, roles, systems, constraints—and run a prompt. Out comes a tailored application guide: a strategic document that maps the framework to your specific context.

  • Role playbooks with scored workflow candidates
  • Complete workflow inventory for each role
  • Quick wins your team can act on immediately
  • Data sensitivity classification (T1–T5)
  • Risk register with mitigations
  • Phased rollout plan with timeline
This is what a consulting firm would deliver in week 4 of an engagement. You get it in an afternoon.
2

Training Materials

~half day

Feed your Layer 1 output into the next prompt. The AI generates role-specific training content tuned to your organisation—not generic worked examples, but examples built from your actual workflows and systems.

  • Per-role learning paths (each person gets content about their workflows)
  • Personalised worked examples using your tools and processes
  • Workshop plans for team enablement sessions
  • Assessment rubrics to measure adoption
Every person on your team gets lessons about their workflows, not a generic CSM's.
3

Your Own Site

~1 day

Fork the entire framework site with your organisation's context baked in. Templates pre-fill with your systems. Worked examples use your workflows. The glossary includes your terminology. The site becomes yours.

  • Branded framework site with your org's identity
  • Templates that pre-fill with your systems and roles
  • Worked examples drawn from your actual workflows
  • Glossary expanded with your organisation's terminology
Your team opens the site and it reads as if it was written for them. Because it was.

Section 3 3 min read

What you need to start

The barrier to entry is deliberately low. You do not need engineering support, a vendor contract, or a procurement cycle. Here is everything you need:

  • 1
    30 minutes to fill in a YAML config file about your organisation—name, industry, roles, systems, regulatory context, and goals.
  • 2
    Access to an AI assistant—Claude, ChatGPT, or a similar large language model. Any tier works; you do not need an enterprise plan.
  • 3
    The framework repo—clone it from GitHub or download the ZIP. All prompts, templates, and instructions are included.
  • 4
    That is it. No engineering. No vendor. No contract. No multi-week onboarding.

What the config file looks like

You are not writing code. You are answering questions about your organisation in a structured format. Here is what you will fill in:

🏢
Your Company

Name, industry, division or business unit. Basic identity so the output reads as yours.

👥
Your Roles

2–5 role descriptions. Job titles, what they do day to day, what their key deliverables are.

🔧
Your Systems

List of tools your team uses—CRM, project management, communication, analytics—with a note on whether each has API access.

🔒
Your Constraints

Regulatory requirements, data sensitivity rules, client-facing restrictions. Anything that limits what can be automated.

🎯
Your Goals

What you want to achieve with automation. Time savings? Consistency? Scale? Quality? This shapes how the framework prioritises workflows and recommends quick wins.

The config file is typically 20–30 lines of YAML. If you can fill out a structured form, you can fill out this file. The prompts guide you through every field.
