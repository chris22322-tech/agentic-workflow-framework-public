Lesson 3 of 8 Stage 2: Select ~20 min

Select Your First Workflow

You have a list of workflows from Lesson 2. Now you need to choose which one to automate first — not the one that sounds most exciting, but the one that will teach you the most with the least risk.

3 / 8
1

Why Selection Matters

~3 min

Not every workflow should be your first agent. If you pick something too ambitious, you will spend weeks debugging integrations instead of learning the methodology. If you pick something too trivial, you will not learn anything transferable.

The goal of your first agent is learning, not maximum impact. You need to understand the full build-test-refine cycle before you tackle your highest-value workflow. Think of it as a training run, not a production deployment.

Key Principle

Your first agent teaches you the process. Your second agent delivers the value.

This means you should optimise your first selection for feasibility and learning speed, not for time savings or stakeholder visibility. Those matter for your second and third agents, once you understand how the process works.

2

The Six Scoring Dimensions

~8 min

The framework uses six dimensions to evaluate each candidate workflow. Score each dimension 1–5 to get a total out of 30. The score is not about precision — it is about making the decision visible and defensible.

⏱
Time Savings
Hours saved per week. Weight frequency over single-occurrence duration: a 1-hour workflow done 5x/week saves more than a 6-hour workflow done quarterly.
BA example: Feature intake triage at 2–3 hrs per occurrence, 3–5x/week = 6–15 hrs/week. Score: High.
🛠
Complexity
Number of steps, decision points, and systems involved. More complex = harder first build. Lower complexity scores higher for a first agent.
BA guidance: Start with workflows that have 5–8 clearly defined steps. Anything above 12 steps likely needs decomposition first.
🗃
Data Availability
Can the agent access the data it needs? APIs beat CSV exports, which beat wiki pages, which beat “ask someone.”
Check: Jira REST API? Confluence API? Roadmap tool API? If the data lives in someone's head, score Low.
🛡
Error Tolerance
What happens if the agent gets it wrong? Workflows where a human reviews the output before it reaches external stakeholders are forgiving.
BA example: Impact assessments reviewed by the BA before reaching the prioritisation committee = High tolerance.
🎯
Stakeholder Impact
Who benefits and how visibly? Workflows that save time AND improve quality score highest. Time savings alone is good; quality improvement alone is hard to measure.
BA example: Consistent, structured impact assessments benefit the prioritisation committee by making decisions faster and more transparent.
⚙
Technical Feasibility
Can you build this yourself, or do you need engineering support? Code-first frameworks require programming skills. Low-code agent builders are self-service.
Consider: What platform are you using? What engineering support is available? Match ambition to your current capability.
Key Insight

For BAs, the dimension that matters most for a first agent is Data Availability. You can work around moderate complexity and limited stakeholder visibility. You cannot work around data that does not exist in a system the agent can read.

3

BA Candidate Comparison

~5 min

Here are five typical BA workflows scored against the dimensions that matter most for first-agent selection. Use this as a reference when scoring your own candidates.

Workflow Time Savings Data Access Error Tolerance Best For
Feature request intake High (frequency) Medium (multiple APIs) High (reviewed) First agent — high volume, clear structure
Backlog grooming prep Medium (per-occ) High (Jira native) High Quick win — simple, API-rich
Sprint velocity analysis Low (may exist) High (Jira data) Medium Already solved by dashboards?
Change request impact High (time cost) Medium Low (political) Second agent — needs HIL maturity
Stakeholder progress report High (time cost) Mixed Medium Third agent — narrative synthesis
Feature request intake — why it makes a strong first agent

Feature request intake is the classic BA first agent. It runs frequently (3–5 times per week), follows a consistent structure (gather context, check duplicates, assess impact, write up), and produces a draft that you review before it goes anywhere. The data sources — Jira, Confluence, roadmap tools — all have APIs, though you may need to integrate multiple systems. The combination of high frequency and human review before output makes it ideal for learning the build-test-refine cycle.

Backlog grooming prep — the simplest quick win

If feature intake feels too large for a first attempt, backlog grooming prep is a lighter alternative. The workflow is almost entirely within Jira: pull stories, check completeness, flag missing acceptance criteria, suggest priority ordering based on dependencies. Data access is excellent (single API), error tolerance is high (the output is a suggestion, not a decision), and the scope is narrow enough to build in a day. The downside: lower time savings per occurrence, so the learning value is moderate.

Sprint velocity analysis — check before you build

Sprint velocity analysis has excellent data availability (Jira captures everything you need) but a critical question: does this already exist? Most teams have velocity dashboards in Jira, Azure DevOps, or a third-party tool. Before scoring this workflow, check whether the problem is truly “we do not have this data” or “we have this data but nobody looks at it.” Automating something that already exists teaches you nothing and wastes political capital.

Change request impact — high value, but not first

Change request impact assessments are where BAs spend the most painful hours: gathering cross-system data, assessing downstream effects, writing up recommendations for governance committees. The time savings are real. But the error tolerance is low — an incorrect impact assessment can lead to bad prioritisation decisions with political consequences. This workflow benefits enormously from human-in-the-loop maturity that you develop by building your first and second agents. Save it for when you understand how to tune agent outputs and set up robust review checkpoints.

Stakeholder progress report — a synthesis challenge

Progress reports are time-consuming because they require narrative synthesis: pulling data from multiple sources and turning it into a story for a specific audience. The data access challenge is real — some data lives in Jira, some in Confluence, some in email threads, some in people's verbal updates. The agent needs to handle mixed-quality inputs and produce coherent narratives. This is a strong third agent once you have experience with prompt engineering and multi-source integration, but it is too ambitious for a first build.

4

First-Agent Selection Criteria

~2 min

Beyond the scoring dimensions, apply these three rules to make your final selection. If a workflow fails any of these, move to your next-highest scorer.

  1. Pick something with a human review step. Your first agent will make mistakes. A built-in review step before any output reaches stakeholders is your safety net. It lets you learn from agent errors without consequences.
  2. Pick something with good data availability. Avoid workflows where the critical data lives in wikis, emails, or people's heads. If the agent cannot access the data programmatically, your build will stall on integration problems rather than teaching you the methodology.
  3. Pick something you do frequently enough to iterate. You need multiple runs to improve prompts, adjust outputs, and tune the agent. A workflow you do once a quarter gives you four learning cycles per year. A workflow you do three times a week gives you over 150. Frequency is your feedback loop.
Practical Tip

If your top scorer meets all three rules, go with it. If it fails one rule, check your second-highest scorer. Do not try to force a workflow that fails on data availability — that problem does not get easier with effort.

5

Self-Check

~2 min
Reflection Questions
Why is “highest time savings” not always the best criterion for your first agent?

Your first agent teaches you the process. If the highest time-savings workflow is also the most complex, you will spend your learning phase fighting integration problems and political sensitivities instead of understanding the methodology. A moderately valuable but simple workflow lets you complete the full build-test-refine cycle, which is the real goal of your first agent. You learn the process on something manageable, then apply that knowledge to the high-value target.

A workflow scores High on time savings and stakeholder impact, but Low on data availability. Should you pick it?

Probably not for your first agent. Low data availability means the build will stall on integration problems — you will spend weeks figuring out how to get data out of systems that were not designed for automation. That is valuable engineering work, but it is not what you need to learn right now. Pick a workflow with better data access for your first agent, learn the methodology, then come back to the high-impact workflow with the skills and confidence to tackle the integration challenge.

What you should have after this lesson

  • Scores for your top 3–5 candidates across all 6 dimensions
  • A selection decision record with reasoning for each score
  • One workflow selected for Stages 3–6 of the methodology
