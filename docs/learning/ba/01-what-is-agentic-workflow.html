Lesson 1 of 8

What is an agentic workflow
and why does it matter to a BA?

You are not about to learn how to code. You are about to learn how to turn your structured analysis skills into the blueprint for an AI agent—one that handles the repetitive parts of your work while you focus on the thinking that actually requires you.

⏲ ~20 minutes
📚 No technical skills required
☑ Ends with a self-check
Section 1 5 min read

You already know what structured process looks like

Think about what you did last week. You probably triaged incoming feature requests in Jira, pulled backlog data for a grooming session, compiled sprint metrics into a velocity report, or wrote up an impact assessment for a change request. Same pattern each time. Same steps, different data.

Now think about how much of that was genuinely hard. Not tedious—hard. The part that required your domain knowledge, your understanding of stakeholder dynamics, your judgement about what matters. If you are honest, the hard part was probably 20–30% of the total time. The rest was gathering, formatting, and assembling.

This framework helps you figure out which of those workflows an AI agent could handle—and which parts you should keep doing yourself. It uses the structured analysis skills you already have.

It is not a tool. It is not a product you are being asked to adopt. It is a methodology: a structured approach for looking at your own work, identifying what is repeatable, and designing a process where an AI agent handles the mechanical parts while you handle the parts that actually need your expertise.

Six stages. You own the first three.

The framework has six stages. They go from understanding your work all the way through to a working, tested agent. Here is the key thing for BAs: you own Stages 1–3 outright. No code, no technical skills. Just clear thinking about your own workflows—the kind of structured analysis you already do every day.

Stages 1–3 are requirements analysis—your core skill set. Stage 4 is collaborative design with an engineer. Stage 5 is build—an engineer codes it on whichever framework your team uses, or you build it yourself on a low-code platform. Stage 6 is evaluation—you write the test cases, the engineer runs them.

By the end of this learning pathway, you will be able to complete Stages 1 through 3 for any workflow you own. The output—a scope document—is your handoff artifact to engineering. It is the BA equivalent of a requirements specification, but for an AI agent instead of a feature.

How is this different from writing a BRD?

A Business Requirements Document describes what the system should do. A scope document describes how you currently do it—every step, every decision, every system you touch—and then draws a line between what the agent handles and what stays with you. Think of it as a process specification with automation boundaries. You are not defining new functionality; you are documenting an existing workflow with enough precision that someone (or something) else can execute the repeatable parts.

Do I need to know how to code?

No. Stages 1–3 require zero technical knowledge. Stage 4 (Design) is collaborative—you work with an engineer, and this pathway teaches you what to bring to that conversation. For Stage 5 (Build), there are two paths: the engineer builds it on whichever framework your team uses (you validate against your scope doc), or you build it yourself on a low-code platform (no code required). Either way, you do not need to write code.

Section 2 5 min read

What is an agentic workflow, in BA terms?

Strip away the buzzwords. An agent is a program that follows a set of steps you define. It reads data from the systems you use (Jira, Confluence, your roadmap tool), applies rules you specify (scoring criteria, classification logic, priority formulas), and produces outputs you review before anything goes anywhere.

Think of it as automating a process flow—something you already understand deeply. Except instead of a human executing each step, an AI agent does the mechanical ones, and you handle the steps that require judgement.

Key concept

An agentic workflow is not a chatbot. You are not typing questions and hoping for answers. You are defining a repeatable process once—with inputs, processing steps, decision points, and outputs—and the agent executes it the same way every time. If you can draw it as a process flow, you can scope it as an agent.

Notice the two pause points. These are human-in-the-loop checkpoints—the agent stops and waits for your review. Nothing goes to the prioritisation committee without your sign-off. The agent proposes; you approve, edit, or reject.

Why two checkpoints instead of one?

The first checkpoint validates the inputs—are the complexity estimate, alignment score, and team impact correct? The second validates the output—is the final impact assessment document ready for the committee? If you only had one checkpoint at the end, a wrong complexity estimate would cascade into a wrong priority recommendation, and you would have to trace the error back through the entire chain. Two checkpoints keep each review focused.

What is "state" and why should I care?

Think of state as a running log that sits between you and the agent. The agent writes what it finds (backlog duplicates, roadmap context, complexity scores) to this log. You read it at each checkpoint, make corrections, and write your own notes. The agent picks up where you left off, using your corrections. In technical terms, this is a "state object"—but you do not need to think about it technically. Just know that everything the agent does and everything you change is tracked, so nothing gets lost between steps.

Section 3 8 min read

What changes about the BA role—and what does not

Let us address the question directly:

“If an agent can triage feature requests and draft impact assessments, what is left for me?”

Everything that makes the assessment useful. An agent can search the backlog for duplicates. It cannot tell you that the "duplicate" it found is actually a different team's take on the same problem and merging them would create a political minefield. An agent can calculate a complexity tier. It cannot tell you that the affected component has undocumented technical debt from a migration that was never finished. An agent can score strategic alignment against OKRs. It cannot tell you that the VP who requested this feature is about to leave, and the priority will shift the moment that happens.

Who does what

The agent handles
  • Pulling data from Jira, Confluence, roadmap tools
  • Searching the backlog for duplicates
  • Calculating complexity tiers from historical data
  • Scoring alignment against documented OKRs
  • Tracing dependency paths from architecture docs
  • Drafting structured assessment documents
You handle
  • Validating that "duplicates" are genuinely the same request
  • Adding context from conversations and relationships
  • Adjusting estimates based on undocumented technical debt
  • Reading the political landscape around priorities
  • Deciding how and when to submit to the committee
  • Stakeholder communication and pre-briefing
The shift

Your value moves from assembling the assessment to interpreting and improving the assessment. The BA who reviews an agent-drafted impact assessment in 30 minutes delivers more value than the BA who spent 2.5 hours building it from scratch—because the review time was spent on judgement, not on Jira queries.

The scope document is your new superpower

Here is the thing most BAs miss: the scope document you produce in Stage 3 is not just an input for the engineer. It is the most precise description of your workflow that has ever been written down. Every step, every decision criterion, every system touchpoint, every error path. When you hand that to engineering, you are not asking them to figure out what you do. You are telling them exactly what to build.

A good scope document eliminates the most common failure mode in automation projects: the engineer builds what they think you do instead of what you actually do. You have been bridging that gap your entire career. This framework gives you a format for doing it.

Section 4 2 min

Quick self-check

Before you move on, take two minutes to think about your own work. These questions set you up for Lesson 2, where you will break your entire role into a structured workflow inventory.

1

Think of a workflow you did last week that followed roughly the same steps as the last time you did it. What was it?

Feature request triage? Sprint planning prep? A stakeholder report? Just picture the steps you walked through.

2

How much of that workflow was gathering and assembling data versus interpreting and deciding?

Be honest. The gathering is what the agent handles. The interpreting is what you keep.

3

Which systems did you open? Jira, Confluence, a roadmap tool, a spreadsheet, Slack?

Each system you open is a data source an agent could pull from automatically. The more systems, the more gathering time you can reclaim.

4

If you got those gathering hours back every week, what would you spend them on?

Deeper analysis? More stakeholder conversations? Getting ahead of the backlog instead of reacting to it?

If you can picture that workflow and feel the weight of its repetitiveness, you are ready for Lesson 2. That is where you will learn how to break your entire role down systematically—using the four-angle method tailored to BA workflows.
