Section 1 8 min read

The four-angle method

If someone asks “What are your workflows?” you will list the obvious ones—the ones with names, the ones on your calendar. You will miss at least half of what you actually do, because the habitual workflows are invisible.

The four-angle method approaches the question from four different directions. Each angle surfaces different workflows. Together, they give you a complete inventory.

⏲ Time-Based

“What do I do every day? Every sprint? Every quarter?”

  • Daily: standup prep, Jira board triage
  • Biweekly: sprint planning prep, backlog grooming prep
  • Monthly: stakeholder progress reporting
  • Quarterly: gap analysis, roadmap planning input

⚡ Trigger-Based

“What kicks off work that was not on my calendar?”

  • New feature request arrives in Jira or Slack
  • Change request submitted to change board
  • Feature enters QA stage (UAT coordination)
  • Executive asks for an ad-hoc status update

📄 Output-Based

“What documents or artifacts do I produce?”

  • Impact assessments for feature requests
  • Sprint planning briefs
  • Requirements specifications (BRDs, user stories)
  • UAT plans and test result summaries
  • Velocity reports with trend analysis

💻 System-Based

“Why do I open Jira? What do I do in Confluence?”

  • Jira: ticket triage, backlog queries, sprint metrics
  • Confluence: architecture docs, requirements pages, decision records
  • Roadmap tool: priority alignment, initiative tracking
  • Slack: gathering context from engineering channels

The point is to surface workflows you do on autopilot. The four angles force you to approach from different directions, catching the workflows that live in muscle memory. If you only ask “What do I do?” you miss the habitual ones.

How is this different from a RACI or responsibility matrix?

A RACI maps people to activities. The four-angle method maps you to your own workflows at a granularity that a RACI never reaches. A RACI says “BA is responsible for requirements.” The four-angle method says “The BA runs 3 distinct workflows within requirements: intake triage (2h, ad-hoc), requirements documentation (4–6h per feature), and requirements review facilitation (2–3h per feature).” That granularity is what you need to decide which workflow to automate.

The four-angle method surfaces the ones that hide: UAT coordination only appears from the trigger angle (“feature enters QA”), and project briefings only appear from the output angle (“what documents do I produce weekly?”).

How granular should my workflows be?

A workflow is a complete unit of work with a trigger, steps, and an output. “Check Jira” is a task, not a workflow. “Sprint Planning Prep” is a workflow—it has a trigger (2 days before planning), steps (pull capacity, review backlog, flag dependencies), and an output (planning brief). If you have fewer than 5 workflows, you are too broad. More than 15, you are listing tasks. Sweet spot for BAs: 8–12.

Pattern to notice

In BA workflows, the line between High and Medium automation potential is about where the thinking happens. Workflows that apply documented criteria to structured data (duplicate detection, velocity calculation, tier mapping) score High. Workflows that require the BA to interpret what the data means in a specific context (gap analysis, stakeholder facilitation) score Medium. If the answer depends on criteria you could write down, the workflow is likely High. If it depends on context you carry in your head, it is likely Medium.

Section 4 Reflection

Pause and reflect

Before moving on, sit with your inventory. Three questions:

1

Which workflow surprised you by being on the list? Something you do regularly but never thought of as a “workflow”?

The habitual ones are often the best automation candidates—precisely because they are so routine.

2

Look at the “Systems Touched” column. Which workflow touches the most systems?

More systems = more gathering time an agent could handle. But also more integration complexity.

3

Where does your judgement matter most? Which workflow would suffer most if someone else did it?

That workflow is where you keep the most control. The agent handles preparation, but the decisions stay with you.

If you have 6–10 workflows in your inventory, you are ready for Lesson 3. That is where you will score each one and choose which to automate first—using structured criteria, not gut feel.
