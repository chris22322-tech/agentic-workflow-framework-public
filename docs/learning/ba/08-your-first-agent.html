Your first agent:
Stages 1–3 in practice

You have learned the methodology. Now apply it. This lesson walks you through Stages 1–3 with a real BA workflow—New Feature Request Intake—so you produce actual artifacts, not just understanding.

⏲ ~20 minutes
✏ Hands-on exercise
📄 Produces 3 artifacts
Exercise Overview 2 min read

What you will produce in this lesson

This is not a lecture. You are going to work through Stages 1–3 of the framework and produce three concrete artifacts—the same artifacts a BA would hand off to an engineer (or use to self-build on a low-code platform).

Your three deliverables

1. Workflow Inventory Table (Stage 1)—five BA workflows broken out with triggers, frequency, time, and automation potential.

2. Selection Decision Record (Stage 2)—three candidates scored across six dimensions, with a justified winner.

3. Workflow Step Map (Stage 3)—five steps of the chosen workflow mapped with inputs, outputs, decision logic, and automation boundaries.

Each exercise section gives you a template, hints to get you started, and a collapsible example so you can check your work. The example uses New Feature Request Intake—the same workflow from the BA worked examples you have seen throughout this pathway.

Work with your own workflows if you can. The templates and hints use feature request intake as the reference, but the exercise is most valuable when you apply it to the BA workflows you actually do every week.

Stuck? Here are five common BA workflows to consider

Backlog Grooming Prep — pull ticket statuses, flag stale items, compile a grooming summary. Biweekly, 1–2 hours. High potential.

Sprint Velocity Analysis — gather sprint data, compute velocity trends, write the report. Biweekly, 1–2 hours. High potential.

Stakeholder Progress Report — pull data from Jira and roadmap tools, compile a narrative update. Monthly, 4–5 hours. Medium potential.

Change Request Impact Assessment — trace dependencies, estimate disruption, produce an impact document. Ad hoc, 3–5 hours. Medium potential.

Sprint Planning Prep — compile capacity, priorities, and dependency flags into a planning brief. Biweekly, 2–3 hours. High potential.

How to judge automation potential

Ask yourself: if two BAs with the same data did this workflow independently, would they produce roughly the same output? If yes, it is likely High. If the output depends heavily on the BA’s personal relationships, political awareness, or interpretive judgement, it is likely Medium or Low.

High: Steps follow documented criteria applied to structured data. Example: duplicate checking against a backlog, computing velocity from Jira metrics.

Medium: Mix of structured steps and contextual interpretation. Example: impact assessment that includes both technical tracing and organisational judgement.

Low: Value comes from live interaction or unique synthesis. Example: facilitating a requirements review meeting, conducting a novel gap analysis.

Calculate your weighted score

Weighted Score = (Impact × 0.30) + (Feasibility × 0.25) + (Risk Tolerance × 0.20) + (Complexity × 0.10) + (Learning Value × 0.05) + (Org Readiness × 0.10)

Scoring guidance: what does a 4 vs. a 5 look like?

Impact: 5 = saves 15+ hours/month with high frequency or an instance multiplier. 4 = saves 8–15 hours/month. 3 = saves 4–8 hours/month. Below 3 = probably not worth automating first.

Feasibility: 5 = all data sources have APIs, all inputs are structured. 4 = one data source requires scraping or manual export. 3 = multiple data quality or access challenges.

Risk Tolerance: 5 = output is reviewed before anyone outside your team sees it. 4 = output goes to internal stakeholders with your name on it. 3 = output affects external parties or financial decisions.

Complexity: 5 = linear flow, no LLM judgement steps. 4 = linear flow with one or two interpretation steps. 3 = branching logic or multiple LLM judgement steps. Below 3 = dynamic routing or heavy conditional logic.

Learning Value: 5 = pattern transfers to 3+ other workflows. 4 = transfers to 1–2 workflows. 3 = useful but specific to this workflow.

Organisational Readiness: 5 = stakeholders are asking for this. 4 = stakeholders are supportive. 3 = neutral. Below 3 = resistance expected.

Example: how New Feature Request Intake scored 4.35

Impact: 4 — ~15 occurrences/month × 1.5 hours saved = ~22 hours theoretical, but the BA review loop reduces net savings to 8–12 hours.

Feasibility: 5 — Jira, Confluence, roadmap tool, and OKR tracker all have REST APIs. Every input is structured.

Risk Tolerance: 5 — The impact assessment is reviewed by the BA before the prioritisation committee sees it. Errors are caught internally.

Complexity: 3 — Linear flow, but duplicate detection requires semantic matching and alignment assessment involves LLM interpretation.

Learning Value: 4 — The “triage, assess, and recommend” pattern transfers to change request assessment, backlog grooming, and sprint planning.

Organisational Readiness: 4 — The prioritisation committee has expressed frustration with inconsistent assessment quality and welcomes a more structured approach.

Weighted: 4.35 = (4 × 0.30) + (5 × 0.25) + (5 × 0.20) + (3 × 0.10) + (4 × 0.05) + (4 × 0.10)

Stage 3 Exercise 5 min

Scope: map five steps of your chosen workflow

Take the winning workflow from Stage 2. Map the first five steps as you actually perform them today. For each step, capture the action, what goes in, what comes out, the decision logic (if any), and where you would draw the automation boundary.

You are documenting how you do it now, not how the agent will do it. The boundary column is your judgement about which steps the agent could handle. Use three tags: AUTOMATE (agent does it), HIL (agent drafts, you review), or MANUAL (you do it yourself).

Hint: how to decide the boundary for each step

Ask three questions about each step:

1. Is this data retrieval or transformation? If the step is “open a system, run a query, return the results,” it is AUTOMATE. No judgement involved—if the query succeeds, the output is correct.

2. Does this step apply documented criteria to structured data? If you could write down the rules and two BAs would reach the same answer, it is AUTOMATE. If you could write down the rules but the answer also depends on context you carry in your head, it is HIL.

3. Does the value of this step come from your personal relationships, political awareness, or live interaction? If yes, it is MANUAL. No amount of data will replace what you know from hallway conversations and stakeholder history.

Example: five steps of New Feature Request Intake

Step 1 — Receive feature request: Detect incoming request from Jira webhook, Slack message, or shared inbox. Input: incoming request. Output: captured request with source and description. Boundary: AUTOMATE (trigger detection, no judgement).

Step 2 — Normalise and log in Jira: Parse request into standard Jira fields; create ticket if request arrived outside Jira. Input: raw request. Output: normalised Jira ticket. Boundary: AUTOMATE (data transformation).

Step 3 — Search backlog for duplicates: Query existing backlog using keyword, component, and semantic similarity matching. Input: Jira ticket. Output: duplicate candidates ranked by similarity score. Boundary: AUTOMATE (flagging step, not deciding step—the BA validates in a later review).

Step 4 — Pull roadmap and planning context: Query roadmap tool and Confluence for strategic context in the same feature area. Input: feature area keywords. Output: related roadmap items and recent planning decisions. Boundary: AUTOMATE (data retrieval).

Step 5 — Estimate complexity: Compare request against historical story point data using four factors: component count, median story points, schema changes, external dependencies. Input: request details and architecture context. Output: complexity tier (Small/Medium/Large/X-Large). Boundary: AUTOMATE (criteria-based—given the same inputs, two BAs would produce the same tier).

Common mistake to avoid

Do not map your workflow as you wish it worked. Map it as you actually do it today, including the messy parts. If you always check Slack for context that is not in Jira, that is a step. If you mentally adjust a complexity estimate because you know the team is burned out, that is decision logic. Honest mapping produces better agents.

Self-check 2 min

Review your work against the quality criteria

Before you consider your artifacts done, walk through this checklist. Each item maps to a quality criterion from the framework methodology. If you cannot check an item, go back and strengthen that part of your artifact.

Stage 1: Workflow Inventory

  • Each workflow has a clear, unambiguous trigger (not “various” or “as needed”)
  • Frequency is specific enough to estimate monthly volume (e.g., “biweekly” not “regular”)
  • Time estimates reflect the full workflow, not just the hard part
  • Automation potential ratings are justified by structure, not by how much you dislike the task
  • You used at least three of the four angles to surface workflows you might have missed

Stage 2: Selection Scores

  • Every score has a one-sentence rationale (not just a number)
  • Impact reflects realised savings, accounting for human review time
  • Feasibility is based on actual data source accessibility, not assumptions
  • You checked for disqualifiers before scoring (core value is not relational or live-interaction)
  • The winner is justified by its overall profile, not just the highest single score

Stage 3: Workflow Step Map

  • Steps describe what you actually do, not an idealised process
  • Every step has both an input and an output—no orphan steps
  • Decision logic distinguishes between “no logic (data retrieval)” and “criteria-based” and “contextual judgement”
  • Boundary tags (AUTOMATE / HIL / MANUAL) match the decision logic—judgement steps are not marked AUTOMATE
  • At least one step is marked HIL or MANUAL—if everything is AUTOMATE, you probably missed the judgement layer

If you checked every item, your artifacts are ready. If you are working with your own workflows (not just the example), you now have a genuine Stage 1–3 pass that you could hand to an engineer or use to start a low-code build.

Next Steps 1 min read

What to do with your completed artifacts

You have finished the BA Learning Pathway. Here is what you can do with what you have produced:

1

Hand off to engineering

Your scope document (Stage 3) is the input for Stage 4: Design. Share it with an engineer and use it as the basis for a design conversation. The worked example shows exactly what a completed scope document looks like.

2

Self-build on a low-code platform

If you want to build it yourself without code, pick a low-code agent builder (see [Getting Started: Choose a Platform](../../getting-started/choose-a-platform.md) for current options) and use the framework’s [Stage 5: Build](../../stages/05-build.md) methodology to translate your scope and design into the builder’s configuration. Your step map becomes the agent’s process flow.

3

Expand your inventory

You mapped five workflows in Stage 1. Your role probably has ten or more. Go back and complete the full inventory. The second and third passes get faster—you already know the four-angle method and what a good entry looks like.

4

Share the framework with your team

The methodology works for any role with repeatable workflows. Product managers, project managers, and customer success teams can all use Stages 1–3 with their own workflows. Point them to the learning pathway that fits their role.

What you have accomplished

You started this pathway knowing your workflows were repetitive. You now have a structured method for turning that knowledge into a blueprint an AI agent can execute. The thinking was always yours—the framework just gave it a format that engineers, tools, and agents can act on.
