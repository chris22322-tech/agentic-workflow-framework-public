Lesson 5 of 8 Stage 4: Design ~25 min

Design the Agent

Design is where your scope document meets the engineering reality. Your job is not to create the design — it is to validate it. Every requirement you defined must land somewhere in the design, or it has been dropped.

5 / 8
1

The BA's Role in Design

5 min

Design is collaborative on the code-first track. The engineer creates the agent flow and memory shape; you validate that your scope survived the translation. These are different jobs, and the boundary matters.

You Own (BA)
  • Validating every scope step maps to an action in the design
  • Defining what each HIL checkpoint shows the reviewer
  • Specifying error handling behaviour (degrade vs abort)
  • Confirming decision logic in action specs matches your scope
The Engineer Owns
  • Agent flow and routing between actions
  • Memory / state shape design
  • Framework-specific implementation patterns
  • Performance and concurrency decisions
Key Principle

You validate the “what” and “why”. The engineer decides the “how”. If you find yourself debating the specific framework’s mechanics, you have crossed the line. If the engineer drops a decision criterion without telling you, they have crossed theirs.

On a low-code track, you may own the entire design yourself — the builder walks you through configuration rather than requiring code. Either way, the framework-level design decisions below are the same.

2

How Scope Becomes Design

8 min

A scope document defines the workflow step by step. A design document consolidates those steps into a smaller number of actions. This is normal and expected. Eleven scope steps do not become eleven actions — engineers group tightly coupled steps, remove redundant handoffs, and optimise for efficient execution.

Here is how the Feature Request Intake workflow maps from scope to design:

Scope Steps Design Node
Steps 1–5
Receive request, normalise data, search duplicates, pull roadmap context, pull architecture context 		→ gather_context
Steps 6 + 8
Estimate complexity, identify impacted teams 		→ assess_complexity
Step 7
Assess strategic alignment against OKRs 		→ assess_alignment
HIL Review
Human reviews all three assessments together 		→ hil_review_assessment
Step 9
Draft the impact summary document 		→ generate_impact_summary
Step 10
Human reviews the final summary 		→ hil_review_summary
Step 11
Submit to governance committee 		→ MANUAL — outside graph

Eleven steps became six graph nodes plus one manual step. That compression is where most BA-engineer misunderstandings happen. The three patterns below explain why consolidation is safe — and when it is not.

Pattern 1: Group tightly coupled steps

Steps 6, 7, and 8 each produce a different assessment (complexity, alignment, team impact), but they are all reviewed together at a single HIL checkpoint. The engineer groups them into adjacent nodes because the review context crosses dimensions — a reviewer needs to see complexity alongside alignment to make a meaningful judgement.

This is safe because no decision logic is lost. Each assessment still applies its own criteria internally. The consolidation affects execution grouping, not decision logic.

Pattern 2: Upstream checkpoint converts downstream HIL to automated

After the human validates the three assessments at hil_review_assessment, the impact summary generation (Step 9) becomes deterministic enough to automate. The human already confirmed the inputs are correct, so the downstream node can run without pausing.

This is the checkpoint cascade principle: a human approval upstream can eliminate the need for a human check downstream, provided the downstream step does not introduce new external data or new decision logic.

Pattern 3: MANUAL steps define the graph boundary

Step 11 (submit to governance committee) stays outside the graph entirely. This is not a limitation — it is a design choice. The graph produces an output (the reviewed impact summary), and a human takes that output into a process the agent has no business touching.

Rule of thumb: if a step requires organisational authority (approvals, committee submissions, budget sign-offs), it belongs outside the graph. The agent prepares; the human acts.

3

Reading a Design Document

7 min

You do not need to understand the framework’s code. You need to read the design document and verify it against your scope. Focus on four things:

1
Step Mapping
Does every step in your scope appear in the design? Walk through your scope document line by line. If a step is missing, it has either been consolidated into another action or dropped. Find out which.
2
Consolidation Logic
Where steps have been merged, does the merging make sense? Check that no decision logic was lost in the consolidation. Each original decision criterion should appear in the merged action’s spec.
3
HIL Checkpoints
Are the right steps pausing for human review? Does the checkpoint surface all the information the reviewer needs to make a decision? A checkpoint that hides context is worse than no checkpoint.
4
Error Handling
Does the design handle the failure modes from your error path register? Every error scenario you documented should have a corresponding design response — degrade, retry, or abort.
Traceability Principle

A good design lets you trace every requirement from your scope to a specific design element. If you cannot find where a decision criterion lives, ask — either it has been consolidated (fine) or dropped (not fine).

Practical Tip

Open your scope document and the design document side by side. Go through each scope step and write the name of the action it maps to. If you finish with any unmapped steps, you have found a gap.

4

Low-Code Tracks — You Own It All

3 min

Some agent platforms are low-code builders where you configure the agent through a UI rather than writing code. On those tracks, design means writing the instructions text, configuring tool connections, and sketching the flow visually. There is no engineer to hand off to — you do it all.

Low-Code Design Mapping

The same scope document produces a different-shaped design when you build in a low-code tool:

  • Your scope document’s decision logic becomes the agent’s instructions text
  • Your data inventory becomes tool connections configured through the builder’s UI
  • Your HIL checkpoints become approval rules expressed in instructions or built-in approval steps, depending on what the platform supports
Key Insight

Same scope document, different-shaped design. The workflow logic does not change — only the implementation medium. If you did the scoping well, the design writes itself regardless of platform.

5

Self-Check

2 min

Test your understanding with these two scenarios.

The engineer consolidated Steps 6, 7, and 8 into one node, but your scope document has different decision logic for each. Is this a problem?
Answer

Not necessarily. Check if the node spec contains all three sets of decision criteria. The implementation can be one node while still applying three distinct logic patterns internally. One node does not mean one decision — it means one execution unit. If the node spec preserves every criterion from your scope, the consolidation is safe. If any criterion is missing, flag it.

You notice the design has no error handling for the case where the OKR tracker returns stale data. What do you do?
Answer

Flag it. Your error path register documented this scenario. The design should address it — either with a staleness check (comparing the last-updated timestamp against a threshold) or by noting that stale data will be caught at the HIL checkpoint where the reviewer can spot outdated OKR references. Either approach is valid, but the design must explicitly state which one it uses.

What you should have after this lesson

  • Understanding of the BA's role in Design — validation, not creation
  • Ability to read a design document against your scope
  • Knowledge of consolidation patterns and when they are safe
  • Confidence in what to push back on (missing steps, lost decision logic, inadequate error handling)
